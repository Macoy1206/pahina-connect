Romeo and Juliet

by William Shakespeare · 1597

Act I: Two Households, Both Alike in Dignity

In the sun-drenched city of Verona, two noble families — the Montagues and the Capulets — have been locked in a bitter feud for so long that no one can remember how it began. Their hatred has poisoned the streets of the city, turning every chance encounter into a potential brawl. The Prince of Verona has grown weary of their violence and has declared that the next person to disturb the peace will pay with their life.

Into this world of ancient hatred steps Romeo Montague — a young man of seventeen, dreamy and romantic, currently nursing a hopeless infatuation with a girl named Rosaline who has no interest in him. His friends Benvolio and Mercutio drag him to a Capulet party, hoping to distract him from his lovesick brooding. It is there, across a crowded room, that Romeo's eyes find Juliet Capulet.

The moment their gazes meet, everything changes. Romeo forgets Rosaline entirely. Juliet, who has never been in love before, feels something she cannot name but cannot ignore. They speak, they touch, they kiss — and only afterward do they discover the terrible truth. He is a Montague. She is a Capulet. Their love is born in the shadow of their families' hatred.

Act II: The Balcony and the Secret Marriage

That same night, Romeo cannot sleep. He returns to the Capulet garden and finds Juliet on her balcony, speaking her heart to the night air, not knowing he is listening. She confesses her love for him, wishes he had any other name, and declares that a name is just a word — that Romeo by any other name would be just as wonderful.

"What's in a name? That which we call a rose by any other name would smell as sweet."

Romeo reveals himself, and they pledge their love to each other with the stars as witnesses. The next morning, with the help of Juliet's nurse and the wise but perhaps too-accommodating Friar Lawrence, they are secretly married. The friar hopes that their union might end the feud between their families — a hope that proves tragically misplaced.

The marriage is a moment of pure joy in a play that will soon turn dark. For one brief afternoon, Romeo and Juliet are husband and wife, their love consecrated and real. But the world outside their private happiness has not changed. The feud still burns. Fate is already moving against them.

✦ ✦ ✦

Act III: The Turning Point — Blood in the Streets

The afternoon of the wedding day brings catastrophe. Tybalt, Juliet's hot-tempered cousin, seeks out Romeo for a fight. Romeo, now secretly Tybalt's kinsman by marriage, refuses to fight him. But Mercutio, Romeo's witty and beloved friend, cannot stand to see Romeo humiliated and draws his sword. In the scuffle, Tybalt kills Mercutio — and Romeo, mad with grief and rage, kills Tybalt in return.

In a single afternoon, Romeo has gone from a newly married man to a murderer. The Prince banishes him from Verona on pain of death. Romeo and Juliet have one night together — their wedding night, which is also their farewell. In the grey light of dawn, Romeo climbs down from Juliet's window and disappears into exile, neither of them knowing if they will ever see each other again.

Juliet's father, knowing nothing of the secret marriage, decides this is the perfect moment to arrange her marriage to the Count Paris — a wealthy, respectable young nobleman. When Juliet refuses, her father's love turns to fury. She is given an ultimatum: marry Paris or be thrown out of the house.

✦ ✦ ✦

Act IV and V: The Plan, the Tomb, and the Tragedy

In desperation, Juliet turns to Friar Lawrence, who devises a plan. He gives her a potion that will make her appear dead for forty-two hours. She will be placed in the family tomb; Romeo will be informed and will come to take her away when she wakes. It is a desperate plan, but it is the only one they have.

The plan fails in the most heartbreaking way possible. The message to Romeo never arrives. Instead, Romeo hears only that Juliet is dead. He rushes to Verona, buys poison, and goes to the tomb to die beside his wife. He arrives to find Paris there, mourning Juliet. They fight; Romeo kills Paris. Then Romeo kneels beside Juliet's still form, speaks his last words of love, and drinks the poison.

"Here's to my love! O true apothecary! Thy drugs are quick. Thus with a kiss I die."

Juliet wakes moments later to find Romeo dead beside her. She sees the empty vial, understands what has happened, and takes Romeo's dagger. The families arrive to find both their children dead. The Prince speaks the final judgment: all are punished. The Montagues and Capulets, shattered by grief, finally end their feud — but the price of peace is the lives of their most beloved children.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: A Story for All Time

Romeo and Juliet endures because it speaks to something universal in human experience — the intensity of first love, the cruelty of circumstance, the way that hatred poisons everything it touches. Shakespeare wrote a play about two teenagers, but he captured something eternal about the human heart.

The tragedy is not simply that Romeo and Juliet die. The tragedy is that they die because of a feud that no one can even remember the origin of — because of inherited hatred, because of bad timing, because of a message that never arrived. Their deaths are the result of a world that valued pride and honor over love and life.

Four centuries after it was written, Romeo and Juliet remains the most famous love story in the English language — a testament to Shakespeare's genius and to the enduring power of a story about two young people who loved each other in a world that would not let them.
