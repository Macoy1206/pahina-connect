Paradise Lost

by John Milton

Overview

Published in 1667, Paradise Lost is John Milton's epic masterpiece and one of the greatest poems in the English language. Written in blank verse (unrhymed iambic pentameter), it retells the biblical story of the Fall of Man — Satan's rebellion against God, his temptation of Adam and Eve, and their expulsion from the Garden of Eden. Milton's stated purpose was to "justify the ways of God to men" — to explain why a good God would allow evil and suffering to exist.

The poem is remarkable for its portrayal of Satan, who is in many ways its most compelling character — a fallen angel of magnificent pride and defiant energy. William Blake famously said that Milton was "of the Devil's party without knowing it," suggesting that Satan's heroic qualities make him more sympathetic than God. This debate about the poem's true hero has fascinated readers for centuries.

Published: 1667 | Genre: Epic Poetry, Religious Allegory | Setting: Heaven, Hell, and the Garden of Eden
Chapter Summary: Satan's Rebellion and Fall

The poem begins in medias res — in the middle of the action — with Satan and his rebel angels lying defeated in Hell after their failed rebellion against God. Satan, formerly the most glorious of angels, surveys his new domain and delivers his famous speech: "Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven." He rallies his fallen angels and they build Pandemonium — the capital of Hell — and hold a council to decide their next course of action.

Satan volunteers to travel to the newly created world and corrupt God's new creation — humanity. He journeys through Chaos and arrives at the Garden of Eden, where he observes Adam and Eve in their innocent happiness. He is momentarily moved by their beauty and goodness, but his pride and hatred of God overcome his better impulses. He resolves to destroy them.

"Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven."
Chapter Summary: The Fall of Man

God, foreseeing Satan's plan, sends the archangel Raphael to warn Adam and Eve. Raphael tells Adam the story of Satan's rebellion and fall, and warns him to be obedient to God and to guard against pride. Adam and Eve are happy in their innocence, but Eve is curious about the forbidden tree of knowledge. Satan, disguised as a serpent, approaches Eve alone and tempts her with the promise that eating the fruit will make her like God, knowing good and evil.

Eve eats the fruit and then persuades Adam to eat it too — not because he is deceived but because he cannot bear to be separated from her. Their eyes are opened: they feel shame, lust, and the knowledge of their own mortality. God pronounces judgment: the serpent is cursed, Eve will suffer in childbirth, and Adam will labor for his food. They are expelled from Paradise by the archangel Michael, who shows Adam a vision of human history — the suffering that will follow from the Fall, but also the promise of redemption through Christ.

"The mind is its own place, and in itself can make a heaven of hell, a hell of heaven."
About the Author

John Milton (1608–1674) was born in London and educated at Christ's College, Cambridge. He was a deeply learned man who read Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Italian, French, and Spanish. He was also a passionate Puritan and political activist who served as Secretary for Foreign Tongues under Oliver Cromwell during the English Commonwealth. He wrote extensively in defense of civil and religious liberty, including Areopagitica (1644), a landmark defense of freedom of the press.

Milton began going blind in the 1640s and was completely blind by 1652. He dictated Paradise Lost to his daughters and secretaries. After the Restoration of Charles II in 1660, he was briefly imprisoned for his role in the Commonwealth government. He spent his final years in relative poverty, completing Paradise Lost and its sequel Paradise Regained (1671).

Themes and Analysis

Free Will and Obedience: The poem's central theological question is why God allowed the Fall. Milton's answer is that God gave humanity free will — the ability to choose good or evil — because obedience without choice is meaningless. The Fall was possible because Adam and Eve were free; their redemption is possible for the same reason.

Pride and Humility: Satan's sin is pride — the refusal to accept a subordinate position. His famous declaration "Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven" is the ultimate expression of this pride. The poem suggests that pride is the root of all sin.

The Heroic Satan: Milton's Satan is one of the most complex and compelling villains in literature. His energy, his defiance, his magnificent speeches make him sympathetic even as his actions are evil. This complexity has led many readers to see him as the poem's true hero — a reading that Milton may or may not have intended.

The "Fortunate Fall": The poem ends with Adam and Eve leaving Paradise "hand in hand" — a surprisingly hopeful image. Michael's vision of human history suggests that the Fall, though a catastrophe, will ultimately lead to a greater good through Christ's redemption. This is the theological concept of the felix culpa — the "fortunate fall."
