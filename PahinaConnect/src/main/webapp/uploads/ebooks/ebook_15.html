The Adventures of Tom Sawyer

by Mark Twain · 1876

Chapter I: Tom and the Whitewashed Fence

Tom Sawyer lives with his Aunt Polly in the fictional town of St. Petersburg, Missouri, on the banks of the Mississippi River. He is a boy of boundless energy, irrepressible imagination, and a genius for getting into and out of trouble. He skips school, fights with other boys, falls in love with every pretty girl he sees, and drives his long-suffering Aunt Polly to the edge of despair — while somehow remaining fundamentally lovable.

The novel's most famous episode comes early: Tom is punished for playing hooky by being made to whitewash a long fence on a Saturday morning. Rather than accepting this as punishment, Tom transforms it into an opportunity. He convinces the other boys of the neighborhood that whitewashing a fence is a rare privilege, not a chore. By the time he is done, he has not only gotten the fence whitewashed for free — he has collected a treasure of trinkets from boys who paid him for the privilege of doing his work.

This episode captures Tom's essential character: he is not a rebel against society's rules so much as a creative reinterpreter of them. He finds ways to make the world conform to his imagination rather than the other way around. It is a quality that will serve him well — and occasionally get him into serious danger.

Chapter II: Becky Thatcher and the Graveyard

Tom falls in love with Becky Thatcher, the new girl in town, with the same wholehearted intensity he brings to everything. He shows off for her, gets himself into trouble for her, and eventually "gets engaged" to her in the way of children — a solemn exchange of promises that is both touching and comic. Their relationship is one of the novel's great pleasures: two children navigating the mysteries of attraction with complete seriousness.

But the novel takes a darker turn when Tom and his friend Huckleberry Finn sneak out to the graveyard at midnight. They witness something terrible: Injun Joe, a dangerous half-breed criminal, murders the town doctor and frames a harmless drunk named Muff Potter for the crime. Tom and Huck, terrified, swear a blood oath to keep silent. But Tom cannot sleep, cannot eat, cannot stop thinking about the innocent man in jail.

The moral weight of this secret — the knowledge that an innocent man is suffering while the real killer walks free — is the novel's central tension. Tom is a boy who loves adventure and mischief, but he is also, at his core, a boy with a conscience. The question is whether his conscience will be strong enough to overcome his fear.

✦ ✦ ✦

Chapter III: Pirates on Jackson's Island

Feeling misunderstood and unappreciated, Tom convinces Huck and their friend Joe Harper to run away and become pirates on Jackson's Island in the middle of the Mississippi. The three boys spend several glorious days swimming, fishing, and playing at being outlaws, while the town below believes they have drowned and mourns their deaths.

Tom sneaks back to town one night and hides under his aunt's bed, listening to her grief. He is moved — but not moved enough to reveal himself immediately. Instead, he waits for the perfect dramatic moment: the boys' own funeral. They walk into the church while the congregation is weeping over them, and the resulting scene of joy and relief is one of the funniest and most touching in the novel.

This episode reveals something important about Tom: he is not cruel, but he is theatrical. He loves drama, loves being the center of attention, loves the moment of revelation. His adventures are partly genuine and partly performances — and he is always, in some sense, his own audience.

✦ ✦ ✦

Chapter IV: The Cave and the Treasure

The novel reaches its climax when Tom and Becky get lost in McDougal's Cave during a school picnic. They wander for days in the darkness, their candles burning low, while the town searches frantically for them. In the cave, Tom encounters Injun Joe — who has been hiding there with his stolen treasure. The encounter is terrifying, but Injun Joe does not see Tom in the darkness.

Tom eventually finds a way out, and he and Becky are rescued. The town celebrates. But Tom cannot rest until he has told the truth about Muff Potter — he testifies at the trial, and Muff Potter is freed. Injun Joe escapes, but is later found dead in the cave, having starved after the entrance was sealed.

Tom and Huck return to the cave and find Injun Joe's treasure — a chest of gold coins worth twelve thousand dollars. The boys are rich. Huck is taken in by the Widow Douglas, who promises to civilize him. Tom promises that Huck can join his new robber gang if he stays. The novel ends with the boys on the edge of a new adventure — which Twain would tell in the sequel, Adventures of Huckleberry Finn.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: The Boy Who Never Grew Up

The Adventures of Tom Sawyer is a celebration of childhood — of the freedom, the imagination, the moral seriousness, and the magnificent irresponsibility of being young. Twain wrote it as an adult looking back at his own boyhood on the Mississippi, and the novel is suffused with nostalgia for a world that was already disappearing when he wrote it.

Tom Sawyer endures because every reader recognizes something of themselves in him — the desire for adventure, the love of drama, the struggle between conscience and self-interest, the longing to be seen and appreciated. He is not a perfect boy, but he is a real one, and that is what makes him immortal.
