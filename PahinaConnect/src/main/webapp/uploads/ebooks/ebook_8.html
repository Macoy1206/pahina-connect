Pride and Prejudice

by Jane Austen · 1813

Chapter I: A Truth Universally Acknowledged

The novel opens with one of the most famous sentences in English literature: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife." With this single ironic sentence, Jane Austen announces her subject — the marriage market of Regency England — and her method — sharp, witty observation of human folly.

The Bennet family of Longbourn estate has five daughters and a pressing problem: their home is entailed away from the female line, meaning that when Mr. Bennet dies, the house will pass to a distant male relative. Mrs. Bennet's entire existence is therefore devoted to the project of marrying off her daughters to wealthy men. Her husband, a man of dry wit and limited patience, finds his wife's obsessions both amusing and exhausting.

When the wealthy and eligible Mr. Bingley rents the nearby Netherfield Park, Mrs. Bennet is beside herself with excitement. At the local ball, Bingley dances with the eldest Bennet daughter, Jane, and is clearly smitten. But his friend, the even wealthier Mr. Darcy, makes a terrible first impression — he is proud, aloof, and makes the unforgivable mistake of dismissing Elizabeth Bennet, the second daughter, as "tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me."

Chapter II: Elizabeth and Darcy — A Battle of Wits

Elizabeth Bennet is unlike most heroines of her era. She is intelligent, witty, and refreshingly unwilling to flatter or defer to those who consider themselves her social superiors. She finds Darcy's pride insufferable and takes a decided dislike to him — a dislike that is, paradoxically, the beginning of his fascination with her. He is accustomed to women who seek his approval; Elizabeth's indifference is both irritating and irresistible.

Their encounters are a series of verbal sparring matches, each one revealing more of their characters. Elizabeth is quick, perceptive, and sometimes too quick to judge. Darcy is intelligent and principled but blinded by his own sense of social superiority. Neither of them can see clearly because their emotions — pride on his side, prejudice on hers — distort their vision.

Into this developing dynamic comes the charming Mr. Wickham, a militia officer who tells Elizabeth a compelling story of how Darcy wronged him in the past. Elizabeth, already predisposed to think ill of Darcy, believes every word. She does not yet know that Wickham is a liar and a scoundrel, and that Darcy's apparent coldness toward him is entirely justified.

✦ ✦ ✦

Chapter III: The First Proposal and Its Aftermath

Darcy, fighting against his own better judgment and his family's expectations, proposes to Elizabeth. But his proposal is disastrous — he tells her he loves her while making clear that he considers her family beneath him and that he is proposing against his own wishes. Elizabeth refuses him with spirit, accusing him of arrogance, of separating Jane and Bingley, and of mistreating Wickham.

"In vain I have struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

Darcy, stung by her refusal and her accusations, writes her a long letter explaining the truth about Wickham and his reasons for separating Bingley from Jane. Elizabeth reads the letter and is forced to confront an uncomfortable truth: she has been wrong. Her prejudice against Darcy blinded her to his genuine virtues, just as her liking for Wickham blinded her to his genuine faults. It is a moment of painful self-knowledge.

✦ ✦ ✦

Chapter IV: Pemberley and the Second Chance

Traveling with her aunt and uncle, Elizabeth visits Pemberley — Darcy's magnificent estate in Derbyshire. She expects to find evidence of his pride and coldness; instead, she finds a house of genuine beauty and taste, and servants who speak of their master with genuine affection and respect. She begins to see Darcy as he truly is, not as she imagined him to be.

Darcy himself appears, and his behavior is entirely changed. He is warm, attentive, and genuinely kind to her relatives — people he would once have dismissed as beneath his notice. Elizabeth realizes that her refusal has had a profound effect on him, and that he has been working to become worthy of her good opinion. Her feelings begin to shift in ways she cannot quite control.

Then crisis strikes. Elizabeth's youngest sister Lydia has eloped with Wickham — a disaster that threatens to ruin the entire Bennet family's reputation. Elizabeth, in her distress, confides in Darcy. He disappears, and she assumes he has withdrawn from her life forever, unwilling to be connected to such a scandal.

✦ ✦ ✦

Chapter V: Resolution and the Second Proposal

But Darcy has not withdrawn. He has been working quietly and at great personal expense to find Wickham and Lydia, to pay Wickham's debts, and to arrange the marriage that will save the Bennet family's honor. He does this not for the family's sake, but for Elizabeth's — and he asks that his role be kept secret. It is an act of genuine love, entirely without self-interest.

When Elizabeth learns the truth, her transformation is complete. The pride and prejudice that kept them apart have been overcome — his pride humbled by her rejection, her prejudice dissolved by his actions. When Darcy proposes a second time, Elizabeth accepts with joy. Bingley and Jane are also reunited, and the novel ends with two happy marriages and the satisfying sense that both couples have earned their happiness through growth and self-knowledge.

Pride and Prejudice endures because Austen's wit never grows stale and her insights into human nature never lose their accuracy. Elizabeth and Darcy remain one of literature's great couples — not because their love is perfect, but because it is real, hard-won, and built on genuine understanding. The novel is a comedy of manners, but beneath the wit and the social observation lies a profound truth: that love requires us to see clearly, both ourselves and the people we love.
