Hamlet

by William Shakespeare · 1603

Act I: The Ghost on the Battlements

Denmark is a kingdom in mourning. King Hamlet is dead — officially from a serpent's bite while sleeping in his garden — and his brother Claudius has wasted no time in seizing both the throne and the widowed queen, Gertrude. The court has moved on with unseemly haste. Only young Prince Hamlet, the dead king's son, remains trapped in grief, unable to accept the world's indifference to his father's death.

Then the ghost appears. On the cold battlements of Elsinore Castle, the spirit of King Hamlet reveals the truth to his son: he was murdered. Claudius poured poison into his ear while he slept. The ghost demands revenge — that Hamlet avenge his "foul and most unnatural murder." With these words, Hamlet's world is shattered and remade. He now carries a terrible secret and a terrible obligation.

But Hamlet is not a simple man of action. He is a philosopher, a thinker, a man who cannot act without first understanding. He is tormented by doubt: Is the ghost truly his father's spirit, or a devil sent to tempt him into damnation? Can he trust what he has seen? And even if Claudius is guilty, what right does Hamlet have to be judge, jury, and executioner?

✦ ✦ ✦

Act II: The Play Within the Play

Hamlet decides to test the ghost's accusation. He arranges for a troupe of traveling actors to perform a play that mirrors the circumstances of his father's murder — a king poisoned in his garden by his brother. If Claudius reacts with guilt, Hamlet will have his proof. This is the famous "play within a play," one of the most ingenious devices in all of dramatic literature.

Meanwhile, Hamlet's behavior has become increasingly erratic. He has adopted a mask of madness — whether truly mad or brilliantly feigning it is one of the great questions of the play. He is cruel to Ophelia, the young woman who loves him, pushing her away with harsh words. He is suspicious of everyone around him, seeing potential spies in every face.

"To be, or not to be, that is the question: Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, or to take arms against a sea of troubles, and by opposing end them."

This soliloquy — perhaps the most famous speech in the English language — captures Hamlet's fundamental dilemma. He is contemplating not just revenge, but the very nature of existence, the question of whether it is better to endure suffering or to act against it, even at the cost of one's life.

✦ ✦ ✦

Act III: The Trap is Sprung

The play works. When the actors reach the poisoning scene, Claudius rises from his seat and calls for lights. His reaction is unmistakable — the guilt written on his face confirms everything the ghost has said. Hamlet now has his proof. But still he hesitates.

He finds Claudius alone, kneeling in prayer. This is the perfect moment for revenge — but Hamlet cannot bring himself to kill a man at prayer, reasoning that a man who dies while praying goes to heaven, and that would be no revenge at all. He walks away. It is a decision that will cost many lives.

In his mother's chamber, Hamlet confronts Gertrude about her hasty remarriage. Behind the tapestry, Polonius — the king's chief counselor — is hiding and listening. When Polonius cries out, Hamlet, thinking it is Claudius, drives his sword through the tapestry. He has killed the wrong man. Polonius is dead, and with him, any hope of a clean resolution. Claudius now has justification to send Hamlet away — to England, with secret orders for his execution.

✦ ✦ ✦

Act IV and V: The Cascade of Tragedy

The death of Polonius sets off a cascade of tragedy. Ophelia, driven mad by grief for her father and by Hamlet's rejection, drowns in a brook — whether by accident or by her own hand, no one can say. Her brother Laertes returns from France burning for revenge against Hamlet. Claudius, ever the manipulator, channels Laertes' grief into a plot to kill Hamlet in a rigged fencing match.

Hamlet returns from his aborted journey to England — he has escaped the execution order and sent the men who carried it to their deaths instead. He arrives back in Denmark to find Ophelia's funeral in progress. His grief and guilt are overwhelming. He and Laertes grapple in the open grave, each claiming to have loved Ophelia more.

The final scene is a bloodbath. In the fencing match, Laertes wounds Hamlet with a poisoned blade. In the confusion, the weapons are exchanged, and Hamlet wounds Laertes with the same blade. Gertrude, unknowingly, drinks from a poisoned cup meant for Hamlet. As she dies, Laertes confesses the plot. Hamlet, finally freed from hesitation by the certainty of his own death, kills Claudius at last. Then Hamlet dies in the arms of his friend Horatio, asking him to tell his story to the world.

Epilogue: The Enduring Mystery of Hamlet

Hamlet has fascinated audiences and readers for four centuries because it refuses to offer easy answers. Is Hamlet truly mad or brilliantly sane? Is his hesitation a moral failing or a sign of his superior conscience? Is the ghost a reliable witness or a deceptive spirit? Shakespeare leaves these questions open, and in doing so, he created a character of inexhaustible depth.

The play is ultimately about the impossibility of certainty in a corrupt world. Hamlet cannot act because he cannot be certain — of the ghost's honesty, of his own righteousness, of the consequences of his actions. In this uncertainty, he is profoundly modern, profoundly human. We recognize in him our own paralysis in the face of moral complexity.

Hamlet endures because it is not just a revenge tragedy — it is a meditation on thought, action, mortality, and the human condition. "The rest is silence," Hamlet says as he dies. But the questions he raises have never been silent in the four centuries since Shakespeare first put them on the stage.
