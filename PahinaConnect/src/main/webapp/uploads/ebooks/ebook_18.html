Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea

by Jules Verne · 1870

Part I: The Mystery of the Sea Monster

In 1866, ships from around the world begin reporting encounters with a mysterious creature in the ocean — something enormous, faster than any known animal, and capable of ramming ships with tremendous force. The creature is spotted in the Pacific, the Atlantic, and the Indian Ocean. Sailors are terrified. Scientists are baffled. The world's navies are mobilized.

Professor Pierre Aronnax, a French marine biologist, is invited to join an American naval expedition to hunt the creature. He brings his faithful servant Conseil and meets the Canadian harpooner Ned Land — a man of enormous physical strength and practical common sense who is deeply skeptical of the whole enterprise. Together, they sail in search of the monster.

They find it — and it finds them. The creature rams their ship, and Aronnax, Conseil, and Ned Land are thrown into the sea. They cling to the creature's back in the darkness — and discover that it is not a creature at all. It is a submarine: the Nautilus, the most advanced vessel ever built, commanded by the mysterious Captain Nemo.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part II: Captain Nemo and the Nautilus

Captain Nemo is one of literature's great enigmas — a man of extraordinary intelligence, vast wealth, and deep, unspecified grievances against the surface world. He has cut himself off from humanity entirely, living beneath the sea in his magnificent submarine, exploring the ocean's depths, and occasionally surfacing to sink warships of a nation he refuses to name. He is brilliant, cultured, and haunted by a sorrow he will not explain.

The Nautilus is a marvel of engineering — powered by electricity extracted from seawater, equipped with a library, an art gallery, and an organ. Nemo has named it after the nautilus shell, a creature that carries its home on its back and needs nothing from the outside world. The submarine is his kingdom, his prison, and his refuge.

Aronnax is fascinated by Nemo and by the wonders of the deep sea. He is given access to the Nautilus's library and laboratory, and he spends his captivity — for that is what it is, however comfortable — cataloguing the marvels he sees. Ned Land, meanwhile, thinks only of escape. The tension between Aronnax's scientific wonder and Ned's practical desire for freedom runs through the entire novel.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part III: Wonders of the Deep

The Nautilus takes its passengers on an extraordinary tour of the world's oceans. They walk on the ocean floor in diving suits, hunting with air guns. They visit the ruins of Atlantis, glowing faintly in the deep water. They pass through a tunnel under the Suez isthmus. They hunt giant squid in the Pacific. They are trapped under the Antarctic ice and nearly suffocate before Nemo finds a way through.

Each adventure reveals more of the ocean's wonders and more of Nemo's character. He is a man of contradictions: he saves the life of a pearl diver he has never met, giving him a bag of pearls; he sinks a warship without mercy. He is capable of great generosity and great violence, and the line between them is drawn by a logic that only he fully understands.

The ocean itself is the novel's greatest character — vast, beautiful, terrifying, and indifferent to human concerns. Verne describes it with a scientific precision and a poetic wonder that makes the reader feel the weight of all that water above, the darkness of the deep trenches, the strange beauty of creatures that have never seen sunlight.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part IV: The Maelstrom

As the voyage continues, Ned Land's desire for escape becomes urgent. He has been a prisoner for months, and the wonders of the deep sea cannot compensate for the loss of freedom. He and Conseil begin planning an escape whenever the Nautilus surfaces near land. Aronnax is torn — he wants to escape, but he also wants to stay, to continue his research, to understand Nemo.

The end comes suddenly. The Nautilus is caught in the Maelstrom — the great whirlpool off the coast of Norway. In the chaos, Aronnax, Conseil, and Ned Land escape in a small boat. They are rescued by Norwegian fishermen. The Nautilus disappears into the whirlpool, and Captain Nemo's fate is unknown.

Aronnax returns to civilization with his notes and his memories, but without answers to the questions that haunt him: Who was Nemo? What drove him beneath the sea? What was the nation whose warships he sank? These questions remain unanswered, and in their unanswered state, they give the novel its lasting power.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: The Vision of Jules Verne

Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea is a work of extraordinary imagination — a novel that described technologies that would not exist for decades, that explored a world that most of its readers had never seen and could barely imagine. Verne's submarine, his diving suits, his electric weapons — all were science fiction in 1870 and science fact by the twentieth century.

But the novel endures not because of its technology but because of its characters — the brilliant, tormented Nemo; the wondering, conflicted Aronnax; the practical, freedom-loving Ned Land. And because of its central question: What does it mean to withdraw from the world? Can a person truly live outside of human society, or does the need for connection always find a way back in? Captain Nemo's answer is no — but the novel suggests that his answer may be wrong.
