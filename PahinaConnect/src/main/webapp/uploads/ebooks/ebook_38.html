Crime and Punishment

by Fyodor Dostoevsky

Overview

Published in 1866, Crime and Punishment is Fyodor Dostoevsky's psychological masterpiece and one of the most influential novels ever written. It follows Rodion Raskolnikov, a destitute former student in St. Petersburg, who murders a pawnbroker and her sister, believing himself to be an extraordinary man above conventional morality. The novel then traces his psychological torment as guilt, paranoia, and the relentless questioning of his own theory drive him toward confession and redemption.

The novel is a profound exploration of the psychology of crime, the nature of guilt, and the possibility of redemption. Dostoevsky, who had himself been sentenced to death (commuted to imprisonment in Siberia) for political activities, understood the extremes of human psychology with extraordinary intimacy. The novel's psychological intensity and its exploration of moral philosophy have made it a cornerstone of world literature.

Published: 1866 | Genre: Psychological Fiction, Philosophical Novel | Setting: St. Petersburg, Russia, 1860s
Chapter Summary: The Theory and the Crime

Raskolnikov is a brilliant but impoverished student who has developed a theory: extraordinary people — Napoleons, Newtons — have the right to transgress ordinary moral laws if their actions benefit humanity. Ordinary people must obey the law; extraordinary people can step over it. He has identified a local pawnbroker, Alyona Ivanovna, as a "louse" — a parasitic creature whose death would benefit society and whose money could fund his education and future greatness.

He murders her with an axe. But her half-sister Lizaveta unexpectedly returns and witnesses the crime, so Raskolnikov kills her too. He escapes with a small amount of money and jewelry, which he hides under a stone. But the murder does not bring the clarity and freedom he expected. Instead, he falls into a fever, becomes paranoid and erratic, and is tormented by guilt — even as he insists to himself that he feels no guilt.

"Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart."
Chapter Summary: The Investigator and the Saint

The brilliant investigator Porfiry Petrovich suspects Raskolnikov from early on and engages him in a series of psychological cat-and-mouse conversations, never quite accusing him but always probing, always suggesting that confession would be better than capture. Raskolnikov is simultaneously drawn to confess and determined to resist.

He meets Sonya Marmeladova, the daughter of a drunken civil servant, who has become a prostitute to support her family. Despite her degradation, Sonya has a profound, simple Christian faith. Raskolnikov is drawn to her and eventually confesses his crime to her. She urges him to confess to the authorities and accept his punishment. He reads with her the story of Lazarus — the resurrection of the dead — which becomes a symbol of his own potential spiritual rebirth.

"To go wrong in one's own way is better than to go right in someone else's."
Chapter Summary: Confession and Redemption

Raskolnikov's theory collapses not because he is caught but because he cannot sustain it psychologically. He is not, it turns out, an extraordinary man above guilt — he is an ordinary man who has committed a terrible crime. His suffering is not the suffering of a Napoleon but the suffering of a murderer. He confesses and is sentenced to eight years in Siberia.

Sonya follows him to Siberia. In the epilogue, after a long period of spiritual resistance, Raskolnikov finally breaks down and weeps at Sonya's feet. The novel ends with the suggestion that his true regeneration — his resurrection, like Lazarus — is just beginning. Love and suffering, Dostoevsky suggests, are the path to redemption.

"The darker the night, the brighter the stars, the deeper the grief, the closer is God!"
About the Author

Fyodor Mikhailovich Dostoevsky (1821–1881) was born in Moscow and trained as a military engineer before devoting himself to writing. In 1849, he was arrested for political activities and sentenced to death. He was led to the execution ground, the sentence was read, and then — in a deliberate act of psychological torture — the tsar's reprieve was announced. He spent four years in a Siberian prison camp, an experience that transformed his understanding of human nature and suffering.

Dostoevsky is considered one of the greatest novelists who ever lived. His other major works include The Idiot, Demons, and The Brothers Karamazov. His exploration of psychology, free will, and the existence of God profoundly influenced 20th-century literature, philosophy, and psychology — particularly existentialism and psychoanalysis.

Themes and Analysis

The Psychology of Crime: The novel's central insight is that crime cannot be separated from its psychological consequences. Raskolnikov's theory assumes that an extraordinary man can commit murder without guilt — the novel demolishes this assumption by showing that guilt is not a social construct but a fundamental human reality.

Rationalism vs. Faith: Raskolnikov's theory is a product of rationalism — the belief that reason alone can determine right and wrong. Dostoevsky, through Sonya's simple faith, argues that rationalism without love and humility leads to moral catastrophe.

Suffering and Redemption: Dostoevsky believed that suffering was not merely a punishment but a path to spiritual growth. Raskolnikov's suffering — both before and after his confession — is the necessary price of his eventual redemption.

The Extraordinary Man Theory: Raskolnikov's theory anticipates Nietzsche's concept of the Übermensch. Dostoevsky's critique — that no human being is truly above moral law — is a direct challenge to the kind of moral relativism that would later be used to justify totalitarian atrocities.
