Anna Karenina

by Leo Tolstoy

Overview

Published serially from 1875 to 1877, Anna Karenina is widely considered one of the greatest novels ever written. William Faulkner called it "the best novel ever written." It tells two parallel stories: the tragic love affair between Anna Karenina, a beautiful married aristocrat, and Count Alexei Vronsky; and the spiritual journey of Konstantin Levin, a landowner searching for meaning in life, work, and love. The novel opens with one of the most famous lines in literature: "All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."

Tolstoy's novel is a vast panorama of Russian society in the 1870s, encompassing aristocratic life in Moscow and St. Petersburg, rural farming, politics, philosophy, and religion. It is simultaneously a psychological study of extraordinary depth, a social critique, and a philosophical meditation on the meaning of life.

Published: 1875–1877 | Genre: Realist Fiction, Psychological Novel | Setting: Russia, 1870s
Chapter Summary: The Fatal Attraction

Anna Karenina travels from St. Petersburg to Moscow to help reconcile her brother Stiva Oblonsky with his wife Dolly, whom he has been unfaithful to. On the train, she meets Count Vronsky, a dashing cavalry officer. Their attraction is immediate and powerful. At a ball, Vronsky dances with Anna instead of Kitty Shcherbatskaya, the young woman he has been courting, breaking Kitty's heart. Anna returns to St. Petersburg, but she cannot stop thinking about Vronsky.

Vronsky follows Anna to St. Petersburg and pursues her relentlessly. Anna, married to the cold, correct government official Alexei Karenin and mother of a young son Seryozha, tries to resist but cannot. She and Vronsky begin an affair. When she becomes pregnant with Vronsky's child, the affair becomes impossible to conceal.

"All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."
Chapter Summary: Society's Judgment

Anna confesses her affair to Karenin, who refuses to grant her a divorce — not out of love but out of concern for his social position. Anna leaves him and goes to live with Vronsky, but Russian society closes its doors to her. She is shunned, unable to see her son, and increasingly dependent on Vronsky's love for her sense of self-worth. Vronsky, meanwhile, begins to feel the constraints of their relationship and throws himself into other interests — politics, farming, art.

Anna becomes consumed by jealousy and paranoia, convinced that Vronsky no longer loves her. She takes increasing doses of opium to sleep. Their relationship, which began in passion, becomes a prison of mutual recrimination. In a final act of despair and revenge, Anna throws herself under a train at a Moscow railway station — the same station where she first met Vronsky.

"He lied to me, and I must find out the truth. I will find it out."
About the Author

Count Lev Nikolayevich Tolstoy (1828–1910) was a Russian novelist, philosopher, and moral reformer, widely regarded as one of the greatest writers who ever lived. Born into Russian nobility, he served in the Crimean War before devoting himself to literature. His two great novels, War and Peace (1869) and Anna Karenina (1877), established him as the supreme master of the realistic novel.

In his later years, Tolstoy underwent a profound spiritual crisis and became a Christian anarchist and pacifist, renouncing his earlier works as immoral. He gave away his property, dressed as a peasant, and advocated for nonviolent resistance to evil — ideas that profoundly influenced Mahatma Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. He died in 1910 at a railway station, having fled his home at the age of 82.

Themes and Analysis

Love and Society: Anna's tragedy is not simply that she falls in love with the wrong man but that society refuses to allow her any legitimate expression of that love. The novel critiques the double standard that condemns women for the same behavior it tolerates in men.

The Search for Meaning: Levin's parallel story provides a counterpoint to Anna's tragedy. While Anna destroys herself in pursuit of passionate love, Levin finds meaning through work, family, and a simple faith in God. Tolstoy clearly favors Levin's path.

Jealousy and Possession: Anna's jealousy is not simply a character flaw but a response to her total dependence on Vronsky. Having sacrificed everything for him, she cannot bear the thought of losing him. Her jealousy is the measure of her vulnerability.

The Train as Symbol: The railway appears throughout the novel as a symbol of modernity, fate, and death. Anna first meets Vronsky at a railway station where a man is killed by a train — an omen of her own fate. Her death under a train completes the novel's central symbol.
