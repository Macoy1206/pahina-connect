Don Quixote

by Miguel de Cervantes · 1605

Part I: The Knight of the Sorrowful Face

In a village in La Mancha, Spain, there lived a middle-aged gentleman named Alonso Quixano who had read so many books about knights and chivalry that he lost his mind. He became convinced that he was a knight-errant — a wandering hero destined to right wrongs, protect the innocent, and win eternal glory. He put on a rusty old suit of armor, named his bony old horse Rocinante, gave himself the grand name Don Quixote de la Mancha, and set out to have adventures.

He also chose a lady to dedicate his deeds to — a peasant girl named Aldonza Lorenzo, whom he transformed in his imagination into the noble and beautiful Dulcinea del Toboso. She knew nothing of his devotion and would have been baffled by it. But for Don Quixote, she was the ideal of feminine perfection, the inspiration for all his heroic deeds.

His first adventure ended badly. He attacked a group of merchants, demanding they acknowledge Dulcinea's beauty. When they refused, he charged them — and was beaten thoroughly and left lying in the road. A neighbor found him and carried him home. His friends and family, alarmed by his madness, burned most of his books of chivalry. But Don Quixote was not deterred.

Part II: Sancho Panza and the Windmills

For his second expedition, Don Quixote recruited a neighbor — a simple, practical, good-natured farmer named Sancho Panza — to serve as his squire. Sancho was everything Don Quixote was not: earthy, sensible, motivated by food and comfort rather than glory. He agreed to come partly because Don Quixote promised him governorship of an island, and partly because he was genuinely fond of the old man.

The most famous episode in the novel occurs when Don Quixote sees a field of windmills and declares them to be giants. Sancho tries to tell him they are windmills. Don Quixote charges anyway, his lance catches in a sail, and he is thrown from his horse. He explains the failure by saying that an enchanter must have transformed the giants into windmills to rob him of his glory. This episode — tilting at windmills — has become a universal metaphor for fighting imaginary enemies or pursuing impossible ideals.

The adventures continue: Don Quixote frees a chain of galley slaves (who promptly stone him), mistakes a barber's basin for a magical helmet, and attacks a flock of sheep he believes to be an army. Each time, Sancho tries to show him reality; each time, Don Quixote finds an explanation that preserves his delusion. Their relationship — the idealist and the realist, the dreamer and the pragmatist — is one of the great partnerships in literature.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part III: The Question of Madness and Wisdom

As the novel progresses, something remarkable happens: it becomes increasingly difficult to say who is wiser — Don Quixote or the people who mock him. Don Quixote's madness is real, but so is his courage, his generosity, his commitment to justice, and his refusal to accept a world without honor and beauty. The people who laugh at him are sane, but they are also often cruel, selfish, and small-minded.

Cervantes plays with this ambiguity brilliantly. In Part II of the novel, a duke and duchess who have read Part I decide to entertain themselves by staging elaborate pranks on Don Quixote and Sancho. They are sane, wealthy, and powerful — and they are also deeply unkind. Don Quixote, in his madness, maintains a dignity that his tormentors lack.

Sancho, meanwhile, is given the governorship of a fake "island" — actually a village — as a joke. But Sancho turns out to be a surprisingly wise and just governor, settling disputes with common sense and genuine fairness. The peasant who was supposed to be the comic foil turns out to have more practical wisdom than the nobles who mock him.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part IV: The Return and the Final Defeat

Don Quixote is eventually defeated in a duel by a knight who is actually his friend in disguise, trying to force him to return home. The terms of the defeat require Don Quixote to give up knight-errantry for a year. He returns home, and there, stripped of his adventures and his purpose, he falls ill. On his deathbed, his sanity returns — he recognizes that he was Alonso Quixano all along, that the books of chivalry were nonsense, and that he wasted his life on foolish fantasies.

But the reader is not sure whether to be relieved or heartbroken. The sane Alonso Quixano is a diminished figure compared to the magnificent, ridiculous, heroic Don Quixote. His madness was also his greatness. His delusions were also his ideals. When he dies, something irreplaceable dies with him.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: The First Modern Novel

Don Quixote is widely considered the first modern novel — the first work of fiction that is self-aware, that plays with the relationship between fiction and reality, that creates characters of genuine psychological depth. Cervantes was writing a parody of chivalric romances, but he created something far greater: a meditation on the nature of idealism, the relationship between imagination and reality, and the question of what it means to live a meaningful life.

Don Quixote endures because his question is our question: Is it better to see the world as it is, or as it should be? Is the man who charges windmills a fool or a hero? Cervantes does not answer this question — he simply asks it, with compassion and humor and profound humanity, and leaves the answer to us.
