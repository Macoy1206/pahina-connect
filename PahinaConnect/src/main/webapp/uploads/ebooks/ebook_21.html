The Great Gatsby

by F. Scott Fitzgerald

Overview

Published in 1925, The Great Gatsby is widely considered one of the greatest American novels ever written. Set in the fictional towns of West Egg and East Egg on Long Island during the summer of 1922, the novel explores themes of wealth, class, love, idealism, and the corruption of the American Dream. Narrated by Nick Carraway, a Yale graduate and World War I veteran who moves to New York to work in the bond business, the story centers on the mysterious millionaire Jay Gatsby and his obsessive love for the beautiful Daisy Buchanan.

Fitzgerald's prose is lyrical and evocative, capturing the glittering excess of the Jazz Age while simultaneously exposing its moral emptiness. The novel is a sharp critique of the American Dream — the idea that anyone can achieve success through hard work and determination — showing instead how wealth and privilege create insurmountable social barriers.

Published: 1925 | Genre: Literary Fiction, Tragedy | Setting: Long Island and New York City, 1922
Chapter Summary: The World of West Egg

Nick Carraway arrives in West Egg, the less fashionable of the two fictional Long Island communities, and rents a small house next to the enormous mansion of Jay Gatsby. Across the bay lies East Egg, where Nick's cousin Daisy Buchanan lives with her husband Tom, a brutish former Yale football star. Nick visits them and meets Jordan Baker, a professional golfer and Daisy's friend.

Gatsby's mansion is the site of lavish, legendary parties every weekend — parties to which hundreds of people come without ever being formally invited. Guests speculate wildly about Gatsby's origins: some say he killed a man, others that he was a German spy during the war. Nick is eventually invited to one of these parties and meets Gatsby himself — a surprisingly young, handsome man with an extraordinary smile and an unsettling habit of calling everyone "old sport."

"In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since. 'Whenever you feel like criticizing anyone,' he told me, 'just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had.'"
Chapter Summary: The Green Light and the Dream

Through Jordan Baker, Nick learns the truth about Gatsby: he is deeply in love with Daisy, whom he met before the war when he was a poor young officer. They had a romance, but Daisy married Tom Buchanan while Gatsby was overseas. Gatsby has spent years accumulating his mysterious fortune — likely through bootlegging — and bought his mansion specifically because it is across the bay from Daisy's home. The green light at the end of Daisy's dock has become a symbol of his longing and his dream.

Gatsby asks Nick to arrange a reunion with Daisy. The meeting is awkward at first, but the old feelings are rekindled. Gatsby and Daisy begin an affair, and Gatsby believes he can recapture the past entirely — that they can simply pick up where they left off five years ago. Nick warns him that the past cannot be repeated, but Gatsby refuses to accept this.

"Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!"
Chapter Summary: Tragedy and Disillusionment

The tension between Gatsby and Tom comes to a head during a confrontation at the Plaza Hotel. Tom exposes Gatsby's criminal connections, and Daisy, faced with choosing between the two men, falters. On the drive home, Daisy — driving Gatsby's car — strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson, Tom's mistress. Gatsby takes the blame to protect Daisy, but Daisy never reaches out to him.

Myrtle's husband George, believing Gatsby responsible, shoots and kills him in his pool before turning the gun on himself. Nick, disgusted by the carelessness of the wealthy, arranges Gatsby's funeral. Almost no one attends — the hundreds of party guests have vanished. Nick ends his relationship with Jordan and confronts Tom, who feels no remorse. Nick returns to the Midwest, disillusioned with the East and its hollow promises.

"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."
About the Author

Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald (1896–1940) was an American novelist and short story writer, widely regarded as one of the greatest American writers of the 20th century. Born in St. Paul, Minnesota, Fitzgerald attended Princeton University but left to join the army during World War I. He married Zelda Sayre in 1920, and the couple became emblematic of the Jazz Age lifestyle he would immortalize in his fiction.

Fitzgerald published four novels during his lifetime: This Side of Paradise (1920), The Beautiful and Damned (1922), The Great Gatsby (1925), and Tender Is the Night (1934). Though The Great Gatsby received mixed reviews upon publication, it has since become a cornerstone of American literature and is consistently ranked among the greatest novels ever written.

Themes and Analysis

The American Dream: Gatsby embodies the American Dream — a self-made man who rose from poverty to fabulous wealth. Yet Fitzgerald shows this dream to be fundamentally corrupt. Gatsby's wealth is built on crime, and his dream is not truly about success but about recapturing a lost love and a lost social status.

Class and Social Stratification: The novel distinguishes between "old money" (East Egg) and "new money" (West Egg), showing that in America, wealth alone is not enough — one must also have the right background and breeding. Gatsby, despite his millions, can never truly belong to Daisy's world.

The Impossibility of the Past: Gatsby's tragedy is his refusal to accept that the past is gone. His entire life is organized around recreating a moment that can never return. Nick's final observation — "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" — captures this universal human tendency.

Moral Decay: Beneath the glittering surface of the Jazz Age, Fitzgerald depicts a world of moral emptiness. Tom and Daisy are "careless people" who "smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money," leaving others to clean up the mess.
