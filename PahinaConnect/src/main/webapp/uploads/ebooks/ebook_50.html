Around the World in 80 Days

Overview

Published in 1872, Around the World in Eighty Days (Le Tour du monde en quatre-vingts jours) is one of Jules Verne's most beloved adventure novels and a classic of world literature. It tells the story of Phileas Fogg, an eccentric English gentleman of extraordinary punctuality and precision, who bets his fellow members of the Reform Club in London that he can circumnavigate the globe in exactly eighty days — a feat made possible by the new railway lines and steamship routes of the Victorian era.

The novel is a celebration of the technological achievements of the 19th century and a portrait of the world at a moment of extraordinary change. It is also a charming adventure story with memorable characters, exotic settings, and a plot that keeps readers guessing until the very last page. It has been adapted into films, television series, and stage productions countless times and remains one of the most widely read adventure novels ever written.

Chapter Summary: The Wager and the Journey Begins

Phileas Fogg is a man of extraordinary regularity — he lives by a precise schedule, eats the same meals at the same times, and has never, as far as anyone knows, done anything impulsive in his life. He lives alone in his house in Savile Row, attended by a series of servants whom he dismisses for the slightest deviation from his exacting standards. On the day the novel begins, he hires a new French valet named Jean Passepartout, who is delighted to have found such a calm and regular employer.

That evening, at the Reform Club, Fogg reads in the newspaper that a new railway line has opened in India, making it theoretically possible to travel around the world in eighty days. He bets his fellow members £20,000 — half his fortune — that he can do it. That same evening, he and Passepartout depart from London. The journey takes them through France, Italy, Egypt, India, Hong Kong, Japan, and America before returning to London.

"The world is big enough for us. No need to seek each other out."
Chapter Summary: Adventures Across the World

In India, Fogg and Passepartout discover that the railway line is not yet complete — they must cross a gap by elephant. During this detour, they rescue a young Indian widow named Aouda from being forced to die on her husband's funeral pyre. Fogg rescues her at great personal risk and she joins their party. In Hong Kong, Passepartout is drugged by Detective Fix — a Scotland Yard detective who has been following Fogg since London, convinced he is a bank robber — and misses the ship to Japan.

The journey is a series of obstacles, each overcome by Fogg's calm resourcefulness and Passepartout's comic energy. In America, they cross the continent by train, fight off a Sioux attack, and Fogg rescues Passepartout from a Sioux village. They arrive in New York to find they have missed their ship to Liverpool. Fogg charters a private steamship, burns the ship's wooden fittings for fuel, and arrives in Liverpool — only to be arrested by Detective Fix on suspicion of bank robbery.

"Anything one man can imagine, other men can make real."
Chapter Summary: The Final Race

Fix releases Fogg when the real bank robber is caught, but Fogg has lost precious hours. He races to London by special train, arriving at the Reform Club at 8:44 PM — apparently five minutes too late. He has lost the bet and, with it, half his fortune. He is calm and resigned. He apologizes to Aouda for having involved her in his adventure. Aouda, who has fallen in love with him, proposes to him. He accepts with characteristic understatement.

The next day, Passepartout discovers the solution to the mystery: by traveling east, Fogg gained a day — the international date line means that his eighty days were actually eighty-one days in London time, but only eighty days by his own reckoning. He rushes to the Reform Club with seconds to spare and wins the bet. He has traveled around the world, won £20,000, and found love — though he insists the journey was worth it for Aouda alone.

"He had, without suspecting it, gained one day on his journey — and this merely because he had travelled constantly eastward."
About the Author

Jules Gabriel Verne (1828–1905) was a French novelist, poet, and playwright who is often called the "Father of Science Fiction." Born in Nantes, he studied law in Paris but was drawn to the theatre and to writing. His collaboration with the publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel produced the series Voyages Extraordinaires — a collection of adventure novels that combined scientific speculation with exciting storytelling.

Verne's novels anticipated many technological developments of the 20th century: submarines (Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea), space travel (From the Earth to the Moon), and air travel (Five Weeks in a Balloon). He was extraordinarily prolific, producing over sixty novels in the Voyages Extraordinaires series alone. He is the second most translated author in the world, after Agatha Christie. His works have inspired generations of scientists, explorers, and writers.

Themes and Analysis

Technology and Progress: The novel is a celebration of the technological achievements of the Victorian era — railways, steamships, telegraphs — that made global travel possible for the first time. Fogg's journey would have been impossible a generation earlier. Verne presents technology as a force for human liberation and adventure.

Order and Spontaneity: Fogg represents order, precision, and rationality — he approaches the journey as a logistical problem to be solved. Passepartout represents spontaneity, emotion, and human warmth. Their partnership suggests that both qualities are necessary for success in life.

The Shrinking World: The novel reflects the Victorian sense that the world was becoming smaller and more connected through technology and trade. Fogg's journey is possible because of the global infrastructure of the British Empire — railways, steamship lines, and telegraph cables that linked the world together.

Love and Adventure: The novel's romantic subplot — Fogg's growing love for Aouda — adds a human dimension to what might otherwise be a purely mechanical adventure. The novel suggests that the greatest adventure is not the journey around the world but the journey toward another person.
