David Copperfield

by Charles Dickens

Overview

Published serially from 1849 to 1850, David Copperfield was Dickens's own favorite among his novels — he called it his "favourite child." It is the most autobiographical of his works, drawing heavily on his own childhood experiences of poverty, his time working in a blacking factory, and his early career as a journalist and writer. The novel follows David from his birth through his childhood hardships, his education, his early career, his disastrous first marriage, and his eventual happiness.

The novel is a bildungsroman — a coming-of-age story — and one of the greatest in the English language. Its gallery of characters is extraordinary: the villainous Uriah Heep, the charming but irresponsible Wilkins Micawber, the angelic Agnes Wickfield, and the tragic Emily. Dickens's prose is at its most warm and humane here, combining comedy, pathos, and social criticism with remarkable skill.

Published: 1849–1850 | Genre: Bildungsroman, Autobiographical Fiction | Setting: England, 1820s–1840s
Chapter Summary: Childhood and Hardship

David Copperfield is born posthumously — his father died before his birth — and is raised by his loving mother and their devoted housekeeper Peggotty. His happy childhood ends when his mother remarries the cold, cruel Mr. Murdstone, who brings his equally cold sister to run the household. David is sent away to school, where he is bullied, and then, when his mother dies, is put to work in Murdstone's wine warehouse in London — a thinly veiled version of Dickens's own experience in the blacking factory.

David runs away to his eccentric great-aunt Betsey Trotwood in Dover. She takes him in, sends him to a good school in Canterbury, and arranges for him to lodge with her lawyer Mr. Wickfield and his daughter Agnes. David flourishes, but he notices the sinister presence of Wickfield's clerk, Uriah Heep — a hypocritically humble young man who is slowly gaining power over Wickfield.

"Whether I shall turn out to be the hero of my own life, or whether that station will be held by anybody else, these pages must show."
Chapter Summary: Love and Disillusionment

David becomes a journalist and falls in love with Dora Spenlow, a beautiful, childlike girl whom he marries. But Dora is utterly incapable of managing a household, and their marriage, though loving, is a practical disaster. David realizes he has made a mistake — that he loved an ideal rather than a real person. Dora herself recognizes this and, before she dies young, tells David that it is better this way: she was not suited to grow old with him.

Meanwhile, Uriah Heep has become Wickfield's partner and is systematically defrauding him. The cheerful, perpetually indebted Mr. Micawber — a character based on Dickens's own father — is working for Heep and eventually exposes his crimes. Heep is arrested and imprisoned. Wickfield is saved. Micawber emigrates to Australia and finally prospers.

"I have always been thoroughly in earnest. I have never trifled with you, or with myself, or with any one."
About the Author

Charles Dickens considered David Copperfield his most personal work. The parallels between David's life and his own are extensive: the blacking factory, the imprisonment of a parent for debt (David's friend Micawber is based on Dickens's father), the early journalism career, and the disastrous first love (Dora is based on Dickens's first love, Maria Beadnell). Writing the novel was, for Dickens, a way of processing and transforming his own painful past.

The novel was enormously popular when published and has remained so ever since. It influenced countless later writers, including Tolstoy, who considered it one of the greatest novels ever written. Its opening line — "Whether I shall turn out to be the hero of my own life..." — is one of the most famous in English literature.

Themes and Analysis

Memory and Identity: The novel is narrated by an older David looking back on his life, and memory — its reliability, its selectivity, its power to shape identity — is a central concern. David must understand his past in order to understand himself.

The Heart vs. The Head: David's two loves — the childlike Dora and the wise Agnes — represent two aspects of love: romantic passion and deep companionship. His journey is one of learning to value the latter over the former.

Hypocrisy and Villainy: Uriah Heep, with his constant protestations of humility ("I am the 'umblest person going"), is one of Dickens's greatest villains — a man whose false modesty conceals ruthless ambition. He represents the danger of mistaking performance for character.

Social Mobility: The novel traces David's rise from poverty to success as a writer, reflecting Dickens's own trajectory. But it also shows the precariousness of this rise and the importance of character — not just talent or luck — in sustaining it.
