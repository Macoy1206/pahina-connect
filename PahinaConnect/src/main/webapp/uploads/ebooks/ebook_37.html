War and Peace

by Leo Tolstoy

Overview

Published from 1865 to 1869, War and Peace is one of the longest and most celebrated novels in world literature. Set against the backdrop of Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812, it follows five aristocratic Russian families — particularly the Bolkonskys, the Rostovs, and the Bezukhovs — through fifteen years of war, peace, love, death, and spiritual transformation. With over 580 characters and a narrative spanning from 1805 to 1820, it is a work of staggering ambition and achievement.

Tolstoy's central argument is that history is not made by great men like Napoleon but by the collective actions of millions of ordinary people. The novel is simultaneously a gripping human drama and a philosophical treatise on the nature of history, free will, and the meaning of life. It is widely considered the greatest novel ever written.

Published: 1865–1869 | Genre: Historical Novel, Epic | Setting: Russia and Europe, 1805–1820
Chapter Summary: The Three Heroes

The novel's three central characters represent different responses to life. Prince Andrei Bolkonsky is a brilliant, proud, and disillusioned aristocrat who seeks glory in war. Pierre Bezukhov is a large, awkward, illegitimate son of a count who inherits a vast fortune and spends years searching for meaning through Freemasonry, philosophy, and eventually simple human goodness. Natasha Rostova is a vivacious, passionate young girl who embodies the life force itself — spontaneous, joyful, and fully alive.

Andrei is wounded at the Battle of Austerlitz and, lying on the battlefield looking up at the vast sky, has a revelation: Napoleon, whom he had idolized, seems small and insignificant against the infinite sky. He survives and returns home, where his wife dies in childbirth. He falls in love with Natasha, but their engagement is broken when she is seduced by the charming scoundrel Anatole Kuragin.

"The strongest of all warriors are these two — Time and Patience."
Chapter Summary: Napoleon's Invasion

Napoleon invades Russia in 1812 with the Grande Armée — the largest military force ever assembled. The Russians retreat, burning their own land to deny the French supplies. Moscow itself is abandoned and set on fire. Pierre, remaining in Moscow, is captured by the French and witnesses executions. He meets a simple peasant soldier named Platon Karataev, whose uncomplicated acceptance of life transforms Pierre's understanding of happiness.

Andrei is mortally wounded at the Battle of Borodino. He is carried in a convoy of wounded soldiers that includes Natasha's family. Natasha nurses him in his final days, and they are reconciled. He dies peacefully, having found the spiritual acceptance that eluded him in life. The French, unable to sustain their occupation of a burned Moscow, retreat in the brutal Russian winter. The Grande Armée is destroyed.

"We can know only that we know nothing. And that is the highest degree of human wisdom."
About the Author

Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910) spent six years writing War and Peace, conducting extensive historical research and drawing on family stories about the Napoleonic Wars. His wife Sophia copied the manuscript by hand seven times as he revised it. The novel was published in installments and was an immediate success, establishing Tolstoy as the greatest living Russian writer.

Tolstoy's philosophical views evolved dramatically over his lifetime. The confident, life-affirming vision of War and Peace gave way to the darker, more moralistic perspective of Anna Karenina and eventually to the radical Christian anarchism of his later years. He came to regard his great novels as morally inferior to his later religious writings — a judgment that posterity has not shared.

Themes and Analysis

History and the Individual: Tolstoy argues against the "great man" theory of history, insisting that historical events are the result of countless individual actions, not the decisions of leaders. Napoleon is shown to be not a master of events but a prisoner of them.

Life and Death: The novel is saturated with death — on the battlefield, in childbirth, from illness — and with the question of how to live in the face of mortality. Andrei's death is the novel's most profound meditation on this question.

The Simple Life: Pierre's transformation through his encounter with Platon Karataev reflects Tolstoy's belief that wisdom is found not in philosophy or religion but in simple, natural living. The peasant's acceptance of life as it is becomes a model for Pierre.

Love and Family: The novel ends with Pierre and Natasha married and raising a family, and with Nikolai Rostov and Princess Mary similarly settled. Tolstoy presents family life — with all its mundane demands — as the highest form of human happiness.
