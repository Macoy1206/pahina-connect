Great Expectations

by Charles Dickens

Overview

Published serially from 1860 to 1861, Great Expectations is widely considered Charles Dickens's finest novel. It is the story of Pip (Philip Pirrip), an orphan boy who grows up in the Kent marshes and is suddenly given the means to become a gentleman by a mysterious anonymous benefactor. The novel traces his journey from humble origins to London society and back again, as he learns the true meaning of wealth, class, and human worth.

The novel is a masterpiece of plotting, characterization, and social criticism. Its gallery of unforgettable characters — the eccentric Miss Havisham, the beautiful and cold Estella, the terrifying convict Magwitch, the loyal Joe Gargery — are among the most vivid in all of English literature. At its heart, it is a story about what it means to be a good person in a society obsessed with money and status.

Published: 1860–1861 | Genre: Bildungsroman, Social Criticism | Setting: Kent and London, England, 1820s–1840s
Chapter Summary: The Convict and the Lady

Young Pip lives with his sister and her husband Joe Gargery, a blacksmith. In the churchyard where his parents are buried, he encounters an escaped convict named Magwitch, who threatens him into bringing food and a file. Pip helps him, but Magwitch is recaptured. Later, Pip is invited to visit the wealthy Miss Havisham at Satis House — a decaying mansion where all the clocks are stopped at twenty minutes to nine, the moment she was jilted on her wedding day decades ago. She still wears her wedding dress; the rotting wedding cake still sits on the table.

Miss Havisham has adopted a beautiful girl named Estella and raised her to break men's hearts as revenge against the male sex. Pip falls desperately in love with Estella, who treats him with contempt. He becomes ashamed of his humble origins and longs to be a gentleman worthy of her.

"I took her hand in mine, and we went out of the ruined place; and, as the morning mists had risen long ago when I first left the forge, so the evening mists were rising now, and in all the broad expanse of tranquil light they showed to me, I saw no shadow of another parting from her."
Chapter Summary: The Gentleman

A lawyer named Jaggers arrives with extraordinary news: Pip has a secret benefactor who wishes to make him a gentleman. Pip is to go to London, receive an education, and live as a person of "great expectations." He assumes Miss Havisham is his benefactor and that she intends him for Estella. He goes to London, where he lives beyond his means, falls into debt, and becomes ashamed of Joe and his humble origins.

Years later, the truth is revealed: his benefactor is not Miss Havisham but Magwitch — the convict he helped as a child. Magwitch has made a fortune in Australia and has spent it all making Pip a gentleman, out of gratitude and love. Pip is horrified at first — a criminal's money! — but gradually comes to love the old man. Magwitch has returned to England illegally, risking death to see his "gentleman."

"We need never be ashamed of our tears, for they are rain upon the blinding dust of earth, overlying our hard hearts."
About the Author

Charles John Huffam Dickens (1812–1870) is the greatest English novelist of the Victorian era and one of the most beloved writers in the English language. Born in Portsmouth, he experienced poverty firsthand when his father was imprisoned for debt and he was sent to work in a blacking factory at age twelve. These experiences gave him a lifelong sympathy for the poor and a fierce anger at social injustice.

Dickens was extraordinarily prolific, producing fourteen major novels, numerous short stories, and extensive journalism. His other masterworks include Oliver Twist, David Copperfield, A Tale of Two Cities, Bleak House, and A Christmas Carol. He was also a tireless social reformer who campaigned for better conditions for the poor, for education, and against child labor.

Themes and Analysis

Social Class and Ambition: The novel is a devastating critique of Victorian class snobbery. Pip's "great expectations" corrupt him, making him ashamed of the people who truly love him. His journey is one of learning that true worth has nothing to do with money or social position.

Guilt and Redemption: Pip feels guilty about his treatment of Joe and Biddy, and about his initial rejection of Magwitch. His redemption comes through his loyalty to Magwitch in his final days and his eventual reconciliation with Joe.

Love and Obsession: Pip's love for Estella is not healthy love but obsession — a fixation on someone who cannot love him back. Miss Havisham's obsession with her jilting has destroyed her life and Estella's capacity for love. The novel distinguishes between genuine love (Joe's for Pip) and destructive obsession.

Identity and Self-Knowledge: The novel traces Pip's gradual self-knowledge — his recognition of his own snobbery and ingratitude, and his eventual understanding of what truly matters. The title's irony is that Pip's "great expectations" lead him to become a lesser person.
