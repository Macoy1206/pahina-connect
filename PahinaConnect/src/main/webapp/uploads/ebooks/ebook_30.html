The Scarlet Letter

by Nathaniel Hawthorne

Overview

Published in 1850, The Scarlet Letter is Nathaniel Hawthorne's masterpiece and one of the first great American novels. Set in 17th-century Puritan Boston, it tells the story of Hester Prynne, who is condemned to wear a scarlet letter "A" on her chest as punishment for adultery. The novel explores the psychological and moral consequences of sin, guilt, and hypocrisy in a rigidly religious society.

Hawthorne, whose own ancestor was a judge in the Salem witch trials, was deeply preoccupied with the legacy of Puritanism and the nature of sin and redemption. The novel is a profound meditation on the difference between public shame and private guilt, and on the destructive power of secrets kept too long.

Published: 1850 | Genre: Romantic, Historical Fiction | Setting: Puritan Boston, 1640s–1650s
Chapter Summary: The Scarlet Letter

Hester Prynne stands on the scaffold in the marketplace, holding her infant daughter Pearl, condemned to wear the scarlet letter "A" for adultery. She refuses to name the father of her child. Her husband, Roger Chillingworth, whom she believed dead, arrives in Boston and witnesses her humiliation. He assumes a false identity as a physician and vows to discover the identity of her lover.

Hester is released from prison and lives on the outskirts of town, supporting herself and Pearl through her needlework. Despite her shame, she becomes a figure of quiet dignity and compassion, helping the poor and sick. Pearl grows into a wild, elfin child who seems to embody the passion of her parents' sin. She is fascinated by the scarlet letter and often points to it.

"On one side of the portal, and rooted almost at the threshold, was a wild rose-bush, covered, in this month of June, with its delicate gems."
Chapter Summary: The Minister's Secret

The father of Pearl is revealed to the reader — though not yet to the community — to be the Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale, a young, brilliant, and beloved minister. While Hester bears her shame publicly, Dimmesdale suffers in secret, his guilt eating him alive. He tortures himself physically and psychologically, but cannot bring himself to confess. His congregation, seeing his apparent suffering as spiritual sensitivity, reveres him all the more.

Chillingworth, suspecting Dimmesdale, becomes his physician and confidant, slowly probing his psychological wounds. He discovers a mark on Dimmesdale's chest — a self-inflicted scarlet letter — and realizes the truth. Rather than expose him, Chillingworth devotes himself to tormenting Dimmesdale, feeding on his guilt. He becomes a figure of pure evil, his obsession with revenge consuming his humanity.

"No man, for any considerable period, can wear one face to himself and another to the multitude, without finally getting bewildered as to which may be the true."
Chapter Summary: Confession and Death

Hester and Dimmesdale meet in the forest and plan to flee to Europe together with Pearl. For a brief moment, they imagine a future free from shame and guilt. But Chillingworth discovers their plan and books passage on the same ship. On Election Day — the day of Dimmesdale's greatest public triumph — he mounts the scaffold where Hester once stood and publicly confesses his sin before the assembled crowd. He tears open his shirt to reveal the mark on his chest, then collapses and dies.

Chillingworth, deprived of his victim, withers and dies within the year, leaving his fortune to Pearl. Hester eventually returns to Boston and resumes wearing the scarlet letter — not as a mark of shame but as a symbol of her identity and her compassion. She becomes a counselor to women in trouble. When she dies, she is buried near Dimmesdale, their graves sharing a single tombstone with a scarlet "A."

"Do I feel joy? I deemed the germ of it dead in me! O Hester, thou art my better angel!"
About the Author

Nathaniel Hawthorne (1804–1864) was born in Salem, Massachusetts, and was deeply conscious of his Puritan heritage — one of his ancestors, John Hathorne, was a judge in the Salem witch trials. This legacy of guilt and moral complexity pervades all his work. He added the "w" to his surname to distance himself from his ancestor.

Hawthorne worked for years in the Salem Custom House before achieving literary success. His other major works include The House of the Seven Gables (1851) and numerous short stories, including "Young Goodman Brown" and "The Minister's Black Veil," which explore similar themes of sin, guilt, and Puritan hypocrisy.

Themes and Analysis

Sin and Guilt: The novel distinguishes between different responses to sin. Hester accepts her punishment publicly and eventually transforms her shame into strength. Dimmesdale's secret guilt destroys him. Chillingworth's obsession with revenge corrupts him utterly. Hawthorne suggests that open acknowledgment of sin is healthier than hidden guilt.

Hypocrisy and Society: The Puritan community condemns Hester while revering Dimmesdale, unaware of his guilt. This hypocrisy — the gap between public virtue and private sin — is one of Hawthorne's central targets.

The Scarlet Letter as Symbol: The letter "A" begins as a mark of shame but gradually accumulates other meanings: "Able," "Angel," even "Adultery" transformed into something complex and human. Symbols, Hawthorne suggests, are not fixed but change with context and interpretation.

Nature vs. Society: The forest, where Hester and Dimmesdale meet, represents freedom from social constraint. The town represents rigid moral law. Pearl, born of passion, belongs to the forest; she only becomes fully human when Dimmesdale acknowledges her on the scaffold.
