Robinson Crusoe

by Daniel Defoe

Overview

Published in 1719, Robinson Crusoe is often considered the first English novel. It tells the story of Robinson Crusoe, a young Englishman who defies his father's wishes and goes to sea, only to be shipwrecked on a deserted island near the mouth of the Orinoco River in South America. He survives alone on the island for twenty-eight years before being rescued. The novel is presented as Crusoe's own memoir, written in a realistic, matter-of-fact style that was revolutionary for its time.

The novel has been enormously influential, inspiring countless imitations (the "Robinsonade" genre) and becoming a touchstone for discussions of individualism, colonialism, religion, and the relationship between civilization and nature. It has never been out of print and has been translated into virtually every language in the world.

Chapter Summary: Shipwreck and Survival

Robinson Crusoe is born in York, England, the third son of a middle-class family. Against his father's advice — his father urges him to stay home and live a comfortable, middle-class life — he goes to sea. He is captured by Moorish pirates and enslaved, escapes to Brazil, and becomes a successful plantation owner. But the lure of adventure draws him back to sea, and his ship is wrecked in a storm. He is the sole survivor.

Crusoe swims to the island and, over the following days, salvages tools, food, weapons, and supplies from the wrecked ship before it sinks. He builds a shelter, makes a calendar by cutting notches in a post, and begins the systematic work of survival. He grows crops, domesticates goats, makes pottery, and eventually builds two houses. His journal — which he keeps until his ink runs out — records his practical achievements and his spiritual reflections. He comes to see his survival as God's providence and his isolation as a punishment for his earlier disobedience.

"I have since often observed, how incongruous and irrational the common temper of mankind is, especially of youth, to that reason which ought to guide them in such cases."
Chapter Summary: Friday and Rescue

After fifteen years alone, Crusoe discovers a human footprint in the sand. He is terrified — the island is visited by cannibals from the mainland who bring their captives to eat. He fortifies his home and lives in fear for years. Eventually he witnesses a cannibal feast and rescues one of the intended victims — a young man whom he names Friday (for the day of the week on which he was rescued). Friday becomes Crusoe's devoted companion and servant, and Crusoe teaches him English and Christianity.

Together they rescue Friday's father and a Spanish castaway. Eventually an English ship arrives, its crew in mutiny. Crusoe and Friday help the captain regain control of his ship. Crusoe finally leaves the island after twenty-eight years, two months, and nineteen days. He returns to England, finds that his Brazilian plantation has made him wealthy, and eventually settles down. The novel ends with Crusoe contemplating another voyage.

"It is never too late to be wise."
About the Author

Daniel Defoe (c. 1660–1731) was born in London and had a remarkably varied career as a merchant, journalist, pamphleteer, spy, and novelist. He was imprisoned and pilloried for his political writings. He wrote prolifically on politics, economics, and religion before turning to fiction late in life. Robinson Crusoe was published when he was nearly sixty years old.

Defoe was inspired by the real story of Alexander Selkirk, a Scottish sailor who was marooned on an uninhabited island in the Pacific for four years (1704–1709) and rescued by a privateer. Defoe transformed this story into a much longer and more complex narrative, adding the religious dimension, the character of Friday, and the detailed account of Crusoe's practical achievements. His other major works include Moll Flanders (1722) and A Journal of the Plague Year (1722).

Themes and Analysis

Individualism and Self-Reliance: Crusoe's survival on the island is a celebration of individual resourcefulness and self-reliance. He recreates civilization through his own efforts, demonstrating the Enlightenment belief in human reason and ingenuity. The novel has been read as a founding text of bourgeois individualism.

Religion and Providence: Crusoe's spiritual journey is as important as his physical survival. He comes to see his isolation as God's punishment for his disobedience and his survival as God's grace. His conversion of Friday is presented as a religious duty. The novel reflects the Puritan belief that worldly success is a sign of God's favor.

Colonialism and Race: The relationship between Crusoe and Friday has been extensively analyzed as a reflection of colonial attitudes. Crusoe immediately assumes authority over Friday, names him, teaches him English, and converts him to Christianity — all without asking Friday's consent. Modern readers often find this aspect of the novel deeply problematic.

The Desire for Adventure vs. Stability: Crusoe's father's advice — to stay home and live a comfortable, middle-class life — represents the voice of prudence and stability. Crusoe's repeated rejection of this advice, and his eventual return to a settled life, traces the tension between the desire for adventure and the need for security.
