Gulliver's Travels

by Jonathan Swift

Overview

Published in 1726, Gulliver's Travels (full title: Travels into Several Remote Nations of the World, in Four Parts, by Lemuel Gulliver) is Jonathan Swift's masterpiece of satirical fiction. On the surface, it is an adventure story about a ship's surgeon named Lemuel Gulliver who visits four fantastical lands. Beneath the surface, it is a savage satire of human nature, politics, science, and society — one of the most biting and comprehensive critiques of humanity ever written.

The book has been read as a children's adventure story (particularly the first two voyages) and as a deeply pessimistic philosophical work (particularly the fourth voyage). Swift himself said he wrote it "to vex the world rather than divert it." Its influence on literature and political thought has been enormous, and its satirical targets — political corruption, scientific pretension, human pride — remain as relevant today as in Swift's time.

Chapter Summary: Lilliput and Brobdingnag

In the first voyage, Gulliver is shipwrecked on Lilliput, a land of tiny people six inches tall. He is initially treated as a dangerous giant but eventually wins the Lilliputians' trust and helps them in their war against the neighboring island of Blefuscu. The Lilliputians are engaged in a bitter civil war over which end of a boiled egg to crack — a satire of the religious wars between Catholics and Protestants. Their political intrigues, their vanity, and their cruelty despite their tiny size are a satire of the pettiness of human politics.

In the second voyage, Gulliver visits Brobdingnag, a land of giants. Now he is the tiny one, kept as a pet by a farmer and then by the queen. The King of Brobdingnag, after hearing Gulliver describe European civilization, concludes that the bulk of Europeans are "the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth." The reversal of perspective — Gulliver as the tiny, ridiculous creature — is Swift's way of showing how human pride looks from the outside.

"I cannot but conclude the bulk of your natives to be the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth."
Chapter Summary: Laputa and the Houyhnhnms

In the third voyage, Gulliver visits the flying island of Laputa, whose inhabitants are so absorbed in abstract mathematics and music that they cannot attend to practical matters. He also visits the Academy of Lagado, where scientists pursue absurd projects — extracting sunbeams from cucumbers, building houses from the roof down — a satire of the Royal Society and the pretensions of modern science.

The fourth and darkest voyage takes Gulliver to the land of the Houyhnhnms — rational, virtuous horses who live in perfect harmony — and the Yahoos — filthy, depraved creatures who are recognizably human. Gulliver is horrified to realize that the Yahoos are humans, and he comes to prefer the company of the horses. When he is expelled from their land and forced to return to England, he cannot bear the company of his own family. He spends his days in the stable, talking to his horses. Swift's conclusion is deeply pessimistic: humanity, stripped of its pretensions, is the Yahoo.

"I am not in the least provoked at the sight of a lawyer, a pickpocket, a colonel, a fool, a lord, a gamester, a politician, a whoremaster, a physician, an evidence, a suborner, an attorney, a traitor, or the like."
About the Author

Jonathan Swift (1667–1745) was born in Dublin, Ireland, of English parents. He was educated at Trinity College, Dublin, and later at Oxford. He was ordained as an Anglican priest and eventually became Dean of St. Patrick's Cathedral in Dublin — a position he held for the rest of his life. Despite his Irish residence, he was deeply involved in English politics and was a close friend of Alexander Pope and John Gay.

Swift was one of the greatest satirists in the English language. His other major works include A Tale of a Tub (1704), a satire of religious corruption, and A Modest Proposal (1729), a devastating ironic essay suggesting that the Irish poor should sell their children as food to the English rich. In his later years, he suffered from what is now believed to have been Ménière's disease, which caused dizziness and deafness. He died in 1745, leaving his estate to found a hospital for the mentally ill in Dublin.

Themes and Analysis

Human Pride and Folly: The central target of Swift's satire is human pride — the tendency of human beings to overestimate their own importance, intelligence, and virtue. Each voyage strips away another layer of human pretension, culminating in the fourth voyage's identification of humanity with the Yahoos.

Political Corruption: The Lilliputians' political intrigues are a direct satire of the Whig and Tory parties of Swift's time. The pettiness of their disputes — which end of an egg to crack — mirrors the pettiness of real political conflicts.

The Limits of Reason: The Laputans and the Academy of Lagado satirize the Enlightenment faith in reason and science. Swift suggests that pure reason, divorced from common sense and practical wisdom, leads to absurdity.

Misanthropy and Pessimism: The fourth voyage is Swift's most pessimistic statement about human nature. The identification of humanity with the Yahoos — creatures of pure appetite and violence — suggests that civilization is a thin veneer over a fundamentally corrupt nature. Whether Swift truly believed this or was using it as a rhetorical device to shock his readers into self-examination is a question that has divided critics for centuries.
