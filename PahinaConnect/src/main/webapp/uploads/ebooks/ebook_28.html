Lord of the Flies

by William Golding

Overview

Published in 1954, Lord of the Flies is William Golding's debut novel and one of the most disturbing and thought-provoking works of the 20th century. A group of British schoolboys are evacuated during a nuclear war and their plane is shot down, stranding them on an uninhabited tropical island. With no adults to guide them, they must organize themselves and survive. What follows is a terrifying descent into savagery.

The novel is a direct response to the optimistic adventure stories of the Victorian era — particularly R.M. Ballantyne's The Coral Island — which depicted boys as naturally noble and civilized. Golding, who served in the Royal Navy during World War II and witnessed firsthand the capacity for human cruelty, believed the opposite: that civilization is a thin veneer over a savage human nature.

Published: 1954 | Genre: Allegorical Fiction, Dystopian | Setting: Uninhabited tropical island, mid-20th century
Chapter Summary: Order and Democracy

Ralph, a fair-haired boy, finds a conch shell and uses it to call the other survivors together. He is elected chief over Jack Merridew, the leader of a choir group who becomes head of the hunters. Ralph establishes rules: whoever holds the conch has the right to speak. He focuses on building shelters and maintaining a signal fire on the mountain top so they can be rescued. Piggy, a fat, asthmatic boy with glasses, is Ralph's intellectual advisor but is mocked by the others.

At first, the boys enjoy the freedom of island life. But the shelters are poorly built, the fire goes out, and the hunters — led by Jack — become increasingly obsessed with killing pigs. The tension between Ralph's focus on rescue and Jack's focus on hunting begins to fracture the group.

"We've got to have rules and obey them. After all, we're not savages. We're English, and the English are best at everything."
Chapter Summary: The Beast and the Breaking Point

The younger boys are terrified by a "beast" they believe lurks on the island. A dead parachutist, mistaken for the beast, fuels their fear. Simon, a quiet, visionary boy, goes alone into the forest and has a hallucinatory conversation with a pig's head on a stick — the "Lord of the Flies" — which tells him that the beast is not an external creature but the evil within the boys themselves.

Jack breaks away from Ralph's group and forms his own tribe, offering the other boys meat and the excitement of the hunt. Most of the boys join Jack. During a frenzied ritual dance, Simon emerges from the forest trying to tell them about the dead parachutist. In their hysteria, the boys mistake him for the beast and beat him to death. It is the novel's turning point — the boys have committed murder.

"Maybe there is a beast... maybe it's only us."
Chapter Summary: Savagery Triumphant

Jack's tribe steals Piggy's glasses to make fire. When Ralph and Piggy go to confront Jack, Roger — the most sadistic of the boys — levers a boulder off a cliff, killing Piggy and shattering the conch. The symbol of civilization is destroyed. Ralph is now hunted by the entire tribe. They set the island on fire to smoke him out.

Ralph runs to the beach and collapses at the feet of a naval officer who has come ashore, attracted by the smoke. The officer is amused to find boys playing war games. Ralph weeps for the end of innocence and the darkness of man's heart. The officer turns away, embarrassed, and looks at his own warship — a reminder that the adults' world is no less savage than the boys'.

"Ralph wept for the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart, and the fall through the air of the true, wise friend called Piggy."
About the Author

Sir William Gerald Golding (1911–1993) was a British novelist, playwright, and poet. He served in the Royal Navy during World War II, participating in the sinking of the Bismarck and the D-Day landings. His wartime experiences convinced him of the fundamental darkness of human nature, a conviction that permeates all his work.

Golding submitted Lord of the Flies to 21 publishers before it was accepted. It became an immediate classic and has never been out of print. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1983. His other notable works include The Inheritors, Pincher Martin, and the To the Ends of the Earth trilogy.

Themes and Analysis

Civilization vs. Savagery: The novel's central conflict is between the impulse toward civilization — represented by Ralph, the conch, and the signal fire — and the impulse toward savagery — represented by Jack, the hunt, and the painted faces. Golding argues that civilization is not natural but must be actively maintained.

The Nature of Evil: The "Lord of the Flies" (a translation of "Beelzebub") tells Simon that the beast is within the boys themselves. Evil is not an external force but an innate human capacity that emerges when social constraints are removed.

Power and Leadership: Ralph and Jack represent two types of leadership: democratic and authoritarian. Jack's appeal to the boys' fear and desire for excitement ultimately proves more powerful than Ralph's rational, rule-based approach.

Loss of Innocence: The boys begin as ordinary schoolchildren and end as killers. The novel traces the stages of this corruption, suggesting that the loss of innocence is not a gradual process but can happen with terrifying speed under the right conditions.
