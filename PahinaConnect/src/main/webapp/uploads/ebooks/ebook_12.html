A Tale of Two Cities

by Charles Dickens · 1859

Book I: Recalled to Life

The novel opens with one of the most famous sentences in English literature: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness..." Dickens sets his story in two cities — London and Paris — in the years leading up to and during the French Revolution, a period of extraordinary violence and extraordinary idealism.

In Paris, a man named Doctor Alexandre Manette has been imprisoned in the Bastille for eighteen years — imprisoned without trial, without charge, without explanation, by the arbitrary power of the French aristocracy. When he is finally released, he is a broken man, his mind shattered by years of solitary confinement. His daughter Lucie, who believed him dead, travels to Paris to bring him home to London.

Doctor Manette's release is the first act of a drama that will span decades and continents. His imprisonment — the result of a terrible secret involving the aristocratic Evrémonde family — will reach forward through time to threaten the lives of everyone he loves. The past, in this novel, is never truly past.

✦ ✦ ✦

Book II: The Golden Thread

In London, Lucie Manette becomes the center of a circle of devoted friends. Among them is Charles Darnay — a French aristocrat who has renounced his family name and his inheritance, disgusted by the cruelty of his class. He loves Lucie and eventually marries her, with her father's blessing. But Darnay carries a secret: he is the nephew of the Marquis Evrémonde, the most callous and cruel of the French nobility.

Also in Lucie's circle is Sydney Carton — a brilliant but dissolute English lawyer who drinks too much and cares too little about his own life. He too loves Lucie, with a depth and purity that surprises even himself. He tells her honestly that he is a wasted man, that he has thrown away his gifts, that he is not worthy of her. But he also tells her that there is one thing he would do for her — one act of sacrifice that would give his wasted life meaning.

Carton and Darnay look remarkably alike — a physical resemblance that will become the pivot of the novel's climax. Carton uses this resemblance to save Darnay's life once, in a London courtroom. He will use it again, at far greater cost, in Paris.

✦ ✦ ✦

Book III: The Storm of Revolution

The French Revolution erupts with terrifying force. The Bastille falls. The aristocracy flees or is destroyed. The streets of Paris run with blood as the revolutionary tribunals send hundreds to the guillotine. The idealism of liberty, equality, and fraternity has curdled into a reign of terror in which accusation is equivalent to guilt and mercy is considered treason.

Darnay returns to Paris to help a loyal servant who has been imprisoned. He is immediately arrested — not for anything he has done, but for what he is: an Evrémonde, a member of the hated aristocratic family. The revolutionary tribunal condemns him to death. Lucie, her father, and their friends are powerless to save him through legal means.

Doctor Manette, whose imprisonment by the Evrémondes gives him a kind of revolutionary credibility, manages to get Darnay released — but only briefly. A new accusation, based on a letter Manette wrote in prison denouncing the Evrémonde family, condemns Darnay again. The past has reached forward to destroy the present.

✦ ✦ ✦

Book IV: The Far, Far Better Thing

Sydney Carton, who has been in Paris watching events unfold, puts his plan into action. He obtains access to Darnay's prison cell, drugs him, and has him carried out disguised as Carton himself. Carton then takes Darnay's place in the cell, knowing that he will go to the guillotine in the morning.

As he waits for dawn, Carton is at peace. He has found, at last, the purpose that his wasted life lacked. He is giving his life for the woman he loves and for the man she loves — an act of pure, selfless sacrifice that redeems everything that came before. His final thoughts, as he goes to the guillotine, are of Lucie and her children, of the future he is purchasing with his death.

"It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known."

These final words of Sydney Carton are among the most celebrated in English literature — a testament to the redemptive power of self-sacrifice and the possibility of finding meaning even in a wasted life. Carton dies, but he dies as a hero, and his death gives life to those he loves.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: The Meaning of Sacrifice

A Tale of Two Cities is Dickens's meditation on revolution, justice, and the possibility of redemption. He shows us the French Revolution in all its complexity — the genuine grievances of the oppressed, the genuine horror of the Terror, the way that righteous anger can become murderous cruelty. He does not take sides; he shows us the humanity on all sides.

But at the center of the novel is Sydney Carton — a man who found his best self only at the moment of his death. His sacrifice is the novel's answer to the question of what gives life meaning: not wealth, not status, not even happiness, but love and the willingness to give everything for those we love.
