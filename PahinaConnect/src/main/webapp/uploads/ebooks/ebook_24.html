Animal Farm

by George Orwell

Overview

Published in 1945, Animal Farm is a satirical allegorical novella by George Orwell. On the surface, it is a simple story about farm animals who overthrow their human farmer and attempt to run the farm themselves. Beneath the surface, it is a devastating critique of the Soviet Union under Stalin and a broader warning about how revolutionary ideals can be corrupted by those who seize power.

The animals of Manor Farm, led by the pigs, drive out the drunken farmer Mr. Jones and establish "Animal Farm" based on the principles of Animalism — a philosophy of equality and freedom for all animals. But as the pigs consolidate power, the revolution's ideals are gradually betrayed until the farm is indistinguishable from what it replaced.

Published: 1945 | Genre: Political Allegory, Satire | Setting: Manor Farm (Animal Farm), England
Chapter Summary: The Revolution

Old Major, a prize-winning boar, gathers the animals and shares his vision: humans are the enemy, animals are exploited, and one day they will rise up and be free. He teaches them a song called "Beasts of England." Shortly after, Old Major dies, but his ideas live on. The pigs — particularly the clever Napoleon and the eloquent Snowball — organize the other animals and develop the philosophy of Animalism, distilled into Seven Commandments, the most important being: "All animals are equal."

When Mr. Jones forgets to feed the animals, they spontaneously rebel, driving him and his men off the farm. The revolution succeeds almost by accident. The animals rename the farm "Animal Farm" and paint the Seven Commandments on the barn wall. For a brief, glorious time, the animals work together with genuine enthusiasm and the harvest is the best the farm has ever seen.

"Four legs good, two legs bad."
Chapter Summary: The Rise of Napoleon

Snowball and Napoleon emerge as rival leaders. Snowball is an idealist who draws up plans for a windmill that would provide electricity and ease the animals' labor. Napoleon opposes the windmill and, at a crucial meeting, unleashes nine dogs he has secretly raised from puppies — trained as his personal enforcers. Snowball is chased off the farm and never seen again. Napoleon declares that there will be no more Sunday debates; the pigs will make all decisions.

Napoleon then announces that the windmill will be built after all, claiming it was his idea all along. The loyal cart-horse Boxer, who works harder than any other animal, adopts the motto "Napoleon is always right" alongside his original "I will work harder." The pigs begin sleeping in beds, trading with humans, and drinking alcohol — all things the original commandments forbade. But the commandments on the barn wall are quietly altered to justify each new privilege.

"All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others."
Chapter Summary: The Final Betrayal

When Boxer collapses from overwork, Napoleon sells him to a glue factory — betraying the most loyal servant of the revolution for a few cases of whisky. The other animals are told Boxer died in a hospital, receiving the best care. The pigs grow ever more human: they walk on two legs, carry whips, wear clothes, and invite neighboring farmers to dinner. The other animals peer through the farmhouse window and can no longer tell the pigs from the men.

The Seven Commandments have been reduced to a single slogan: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others." The revolution has come full circle. The oppressed have become the oppressors, and the animals' condition is no better — perhaps worse — than it was under Mr. Jones.

"The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which."
About the Author

George Orwell (1903–1950) wrote Animal Farm during World War II, completing it in 1943, but struggled to find a publisher because of its anti-Soviet content at a time when the USSR was Britain's ally. When it was finally published in 1945, it became an immediate success. Orwell intended the book as a critique not just of Stalin but of all forms of political corruption and the betrayal of socialist ideals.

Orwell was himself a democratic socialist who believed deeply in equality and human dignity. His experiences fighting in the Spanish Civil War, where he witnessed Stalinist agents purging fellow leftists, convinced him that the Soviet Union had betrayed the ideals of the Russian Revolution. Animal Farm was his attempt to expose this betrayal in a form accessible to ordinary readers.

Themes and Analysis

Corruption of Power: The central theme is how power corrupts even those who begin with the best intentions. The pigs start as liberators and end as tyrants, demonstrating that the problem is not who holds power but the nature of power itself.

Propaganda and Manipulation: Squealer, Napoleon's propagandist, represents the role of media and rhetoric in maintaining authoritarian control. He rewrites history, manipulates statistics, and uses fear to keep the animals in line.

The Betrayal of Revolution: Orwell shows how revolutions can be hijacked by those who are most organized and ruthless. The animals' revolution fails not because the idea was wrong but because the pigs were willing to use any means to maintain their power.

Ignorance and Apathy: The other animals — particularly the sheep, who mindlessly repeat slogans, and Boxer, who works hard but never questions — represent the danger of political ignorance and blind loyalty.
