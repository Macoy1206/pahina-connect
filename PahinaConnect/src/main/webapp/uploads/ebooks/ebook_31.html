Moby Dick

by Herman Melville

Overview

Published in 1851, Moby-Dick; or, The Whale is Herman Melville's epic masterpiece — a sprawling, encyclopedic novel about the obsessive quest of Captain Ahab to hunt down the white sperm whale that bit off his leg. Narrated by the sailor Ishmael, the novel is simultaneously an adventure story, a philosophical meditation, a natural history of whales, and a profound exploration of obsession, fate, and the nature of evil.

Though it was a commercial failure during Melville's lifetime, Moby-Dick is now universally recognized as one of the greatest novels in the English language. Its opening line — "Call me Ishmael" — is one of the most famous in all of literature. The white whale has become a universal symbol of the unknowable, the unattainable, and the destructive power of obsession.

Published: 1851 | Genre: Adventure, Philosophical Novel | Setting: The seas of the world, 1840s
Chapter Summary: The Pequod Sets Sail

Ishmael, a young man seeking adventure and escape from the "damp, drizzly November" in his soul, travels to New Bedford and then Nantucket to sign on to a whaling ship. He befriends Queequeg, a tattooed South Sea Islander harpooner, and they sign on together to the Pequod, a weathered whaling ship owned by Captains Peleg and Bildad. The mysterious Captain Ahab does not appear until the ship is well out to sea.

When Ahab finally emerges on deck, he is a terrifying figure — a tall, powerful man with a white scar running down his face and a leg made of whale ivory. He nails a gold doubloon to the mast and announces that the ship's true purpose is to hunt and kill Moby Dick, the white whale that took his leg on a previous voyage. Most of the crew is swept up in his charismatic fury. Only Starbuck, the first mate, objects — but he lacks the will to stop Ahab.

"Call me Ishmael."
Chapter Summary: The Chase

The Pequod sails around the world in pursuit of Moby Dick. Melville interrupts the narrative with long, digressive chapters on the anatomy of whales, the history of whaling, the symbolism of whiteness, and the philosophy of fate. These digressions are not mere padding but are essential to the novel's meaning — they establish the whale as something vast, unknowable, and beyond human comprehension.

After months at sea, the Pequod finally encounters Moby Dick. The chase lasts three days. On the first day, Ahab harpoons the whale but is dragged overboard by the line and barely survives. On the second day, the whale destroys several of the Pequod's boats. On the third day, Moby Dick turns and rams the Pequod itself, sinking it. Ahab, tangled in a harpoon line, is dragged to his death by the whale. The ship sinks, taking all hands with it except Ishmael, who survives by clinging to Queequeg's coffin.

"To the last I grapple with thee; from hell's heart I stab at thee; for hate's sake I spit my last breath at thee."
About the Author

Herman Melville (1819–1891) was born in New York City and went to sea as a young man, serving on merchant ships and a whaling vessel. His experiences at sea provided the raw material for his early novels, which were popular adventure stories. Moby-Dick, his most ambitious work, was a commercial failure and effectively ended his career as a novelist.

Melville spent the last decades of his life working as a customs inspector in New York, largely forgotten by the literary world. He died in 1891 with only a small obituary. It was not until the 1920s that scholars rediscovered Moby-Dick and recognized it as a masterpiece. Today Melville is considered one of the greatest American writers.

Themes and Analysis

Obsession and Destruction: Ahab's monomaniacal pursuit of Moby Dick is the novel's central drama. His obsession destroys not only himself but everyone around him. Melville shows how a single person's madness can drag an entire community to ruin.

The Unknowable: The white whale represents something beyond human understanding — God, fate, nature, evil, or simply the indifferent universe. Ahab's mistake is to project human meaning onto something that has none, to see the whale as his personal enemy when it is simply a whale.

Democracy and Hierarchy: The Pequod is a microcosm of American society, with its diverse crew of different races and nationalities. Melville explores the tension between democratic equality and the hierarchical authority of the captain.

Fate and Free Will: The novel is deeply concerned with whether human beings have any control over their destinies. Ahab believes he can defy fate; the novel suggests he cannot. Yet his defiance, however futile, has a kind of tragic grandeur.
