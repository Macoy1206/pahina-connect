Oliver Twist

by Charles Dickens

Overview

Published serially from 1837 to 1839, Oliver Twist was Charles Dickens's second novel and the first in the English language to have a child as its protagonist. It tells the story of Oliver Twist, an orphan born in a workhouse who endures a series of hardships before eventually finding his true identity and a loving home. The novel is a fierce attack on the Poor Law of 1834 and the brutal treatment of the poor in Victorian England.

The novel introduced some of Dickens's most memorable characters: the villainous Fagin, who runs a school for pickpockets; the brutal Bill Sikes; the tragic Nancy; and the Artful Dodger. It remains one of the most widely read and adapted works in English literature, inspiring countless stage productions, films, and the beloved musical Oliver!

Published: 1837–1839 | Genre: Social Novel, Crime Fiction | Setting: London and the English countryside, 1830s
Chapter Summary: The Workhouse

Oliver Twist is born in a workhouse to a woman who dies immediately after his birth. He grows up in the workhouse system, half-starved and mistreated. At the age of nine, he is sent to a branch workhouse where the boys are fed gruel — barely enough to survive. In the novel's most famous scene, Oliver is chosen by lot to ask for more food. He approaches the master and says, "Please, sir, I want some more." The horrified master reports this outrage to the board, and Oliver is immediately put up for sale.

Oliver is apprenticed to an undertaker named Sowerberry, where he is bullied by the older apprentice Noah Claypole. After a fight, Oliver runs away to London, walking the entire seventy miles. Near the city, he meets the Artful Dodger — a cheerful young pickpocket who takes him to Fagin's den.

"Please, sir, I want some more."
Chapter Summary: Fagin's Den

Fagin is an elderly Jewish criminal who runs a gang of child pickpockets. He takes Oliver in, feeds him, and trains him in the art of picking pockets — though Oliver doesn't initially understand what he's being taught. When Oliver is taken on his first job with the Dodger and another boy named Charley Bates, he watches in horror as they pick a gentleman's pocket. When the gentleman — Mr. Brownlow — notices his wallet is gone, Oliver runs and is caught, though the real thieves escape.

Mr. Brownlow, struck by Oliver's resemblance to a portrait in his house, takes him in and treats him kindly. Oliver is happy for the first time in his life. But Fagin, afraid Oliver will betray them, sends Bill Sikes and his girlfriend Nancy to recapture him. Oliver is dragged back to Fagin's den.

"The law is a ass — a idiot."
Chapter Summary: Nancy's Sacrifice

Nancy, despite being Sikes's abused girlfriend and a member of Fagin's gang, has a good heart. She secretly contacts Rose Maylie, a young woman who has befriended Oliver, and reveals that Monks — Oliver's half-brother — is conspiring with Fagin to destroy Oliver's reputation and prevent him from claiming his inheritance. Nancy refuses to betray Fagin entirely but gives enough information to save Oliver.

Fagin discovers Nancy's betrayal and tells Sikes. In a fit of rage, Sikes murders Nancy. Horrified by what he has done, he flees London but is haunted by her eyes. He accidentally hangs himself while trying to escape a mob. Fagin is arrested, tried, and sentenced to hang. Monks is exposed and forced to share Oliver's inheritance. Oliver is adopted by Mr. Brownlow and finally has a home and a family.

"I am drawn back to the old life. I try to leave it, but I cannot."
About the Author

Charles Dickens (1812–1870) drew on his own childhood experiences of poverty and hardship in writing Oliver Twist. His father's imprisonment for debt and his own time working in a blacking factory gave him firsthand knowledge of the misery he depicted. The novel was a direct attack on the New Poor Law of 1834, which Dickens believed was cruel and inhumane.

Dickens was also responding to the "Newgate novels" — popular crime fiction that romanticized criminals. He wanted to show the reality of criminal life: its squalor, its violence, and its destruction of innocent lives. The novel's depiction of London's criminal underworld was shocking to Victorian readers and remains vivid today.

Themes and Analysis

Social Injustice: The novel is a passionate indictment of the Victorian Poor Law system, which treated poverty as a moral failing and subjected the poor to deliberate degradation. Dickens shows that poverty is a social condition, not a personal one.

Innocence and Corruption: Oliver's essential goodness — his refusal to be corrupted despite everything — is central to the novel. Dickens believed in the innate goodness of children and the corrupting influence of a cruel environment.

Crime and Society: The novel shows how society creates criminals by denying the poor any legitimate means of survival. Fagin's gang is not simply evil — it is a response to a society that has abandoned its most vulnerable members.

Identity and Belonging: Oliver's search for his true identity and a place where he belongs drives the plot. His eventual discovery of his origins and his adoption by Mr. Brownlow represents the fulfillment of the basic human need for family and belonging.
