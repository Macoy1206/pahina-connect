Les Miserables

by Victor Hugo · 1862

Part I: Jean Valjean — A Man Transformed

Jean Valjean spent nineteen years in the prison galleys — five years for stealing a loaf of bread to feed his starving nephew, and fourteen more years for repeated escape attempts. When he is finally released, he carries a yellow passport that marks him as a former convict, and every door is closed to him. No inn will take him in, no employer will hire him. He is a free man in name only; in practice, he is still a prisoner of society's judgment.

Then he meets Bishop Myriel — a man of genuine Christian charity who takes Valjean in, feeds him, and gives him a bed. In the night, Valjean steals the bishop's silver and flees. When the police catch him and bring him back, the bishop does something extraordinary: he tells the police that he gave Valjean the silver as a gift, and then gives him two silver candlesticks as well. "Do not forget," the bishop tells Valjean quietly, "that you have promised me to use this silver to become an honest man."

This act of grace transforms Valjean. He becomes a new man — literally, taking a new name and a new identity. He builds a factory, becomes a successful businessman, and eventually becomes the mayor of a small town. He is generous, just, and beloved. But his past is never truly behind him.

Part II: Fantine and Cosette

Fantine is a young woman who was abandoned by her lover and left with an illegitimate daughter, Cosette. Unable to care for the child herself, she leaves Cosette with the Thenardiers — an innkeeper and his wife who promise to care for her. In reality, they treat Cosette as a servant while spoiling their own daughters. Fantine works in Valjean's factory, sending money to the Thenardiers, not knowing how her daughter is being treated.

When Fantine's past is discovered, she loses her job. Desperate, she sells her hair, then her teeth, then herself. She becomes a prostitute to pay for Cosette's care. When she is arrested, Valjean — now Mayor Madeleine — intervenes and takes responsibility for her. He promises to find Cosette and bring her to Fantine. But Fantine dies before he can fulfill his promise, and Valjean carries the weight of that unfulfilled promise for the rest of his life.

He finds Cosette with the Thenardiers, pays an enormous sum to free her, and takes her away. The little girl who has known nothing but cruelty and neglect slowly learns, in Valjean's care, what it means to be loved. Their relationship — the former convict and the orphaned child — is the emotional heart of the novel.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part III: Inspector Javert — The Law Without Mercy

Inspector Javert is Valjean's nemesis — a man who has devoted his life to the law with the same absolute commitment that Valjean has devoted his life to goodness. For Javert, the law is not a means to justice; it is justice itself. A man who has broken the law is forever a criminal; redemption is not possible; mercy is weakness. He has been hunting Valjean for years.

The confrontation between Valjean and Javert is one of the great conflicts in literature — not between good and evil, but between two different visions of justice. Valjean represents the possibility of redemption, the idea that a person is more than their worst act. Javert represents the law's demand for accountability, the idea that society cannot function if criminals can simply reinvent themselves.

When Valjean saves Javert's life during the Paris uprising of 1832, Javert is faced with an impossible situation. His entire worldview depends on Valjean being irredeemably evil. But the man who saved his life is not evil — he is good. Javert cannot reconcile this with his beliefs, and the contradiction destroys him.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part IV: The Barricades of Paris

The novel reaches its climax during the Paris uprising of June 1832, when young idealists build barricades in the streets and fight for a republic. Among them is Marius Pontmercy, a young man who has fallen desperately in love with Cosette. Valjean, who has raised Cosette as his own daughter, is torn between his love for her and his fear of losing her to another man.

Marius is wounded at the barricades. Valjean carries him through the Paris sewers — a long, exhausting, dangerous journey through the city's underground — to safety. It is an act of extraordinary courage and selflessness, performed for a young man Valjean barely knows, simply because Cosette loves him.

Marius and Cosette marry. Valjean, fearing that his criminal past will taint their happiness, confesses the truth to Marius and withdraws from their lives. He is alone again, as he was at the beginning — but this time, his solitude is chosen, a final act of love for the daughter he raised.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part V: The Final Redemption

Valjean dies peacefully, with Cosette and Marius at his side. He dies as he lived in his later years — with grace, with love, and with the quiet certainty that he has done what was right. The silver candlesticks the bishop gave him are on the table beside him, the symbol of the grace that transformed his life.

Les Miserables is Victor Hugo's great argument that human beings are capable of redemption, that society's judgment is not the final word on a person's worth, and that love — in all its forms — is the force that makes redemption possible. It is a novel of enormous scope and ambition, but its heart is simple: a man who was shown mercy learned to show mercy to others, and in doing so, became truly free.
