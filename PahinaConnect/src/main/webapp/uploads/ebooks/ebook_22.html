To Kill a Mockingbird

by Harper Lee

Overview

Published in 1960, To Kill a Mockingbird won the Pulitzer Prize in 1961 and has become a landmark of American literature. Set in the fictional town of Maycomb, Alabama, during the 1930s, the novel is narrated by Jean Louise "Scout" Finch, a young girl who grows up witnessing racial injustice and moral courage in the Deep South. Her father, Atticus Finch, is a lawyer appointed to defend Tom Robinson, a Black man falsely accused of raping a white woman.

The novel is both a coming-of-age story and a powerful indictment of racial prejudice. Through Scout's innocent eyes, readers see the ugliness of racism and the rare beauty of moral integrity. The title refers to Atticus's teaching that it is a sin to kill a mockingbird — a creature that does nothing but make music and bring joy — a metaphor for innocent people destroyed by society's cruelty.

Published: 1960 | Genre: Southern Gothic, Coming-of-Age | Setting: Maycomb, Alabama, 1930s
Chapter Summary: Childhood in Maycomb

Scout Finch lives with her older brother Jem and their widowed father Atticus in Maycomb, a tired old town where everyone knows everyone. Their summer neighbor, a boy named Dill Harris, becomes their fast friend, and together the three children become fascinated with the mysterious Boo Radley — a reclusive neighbor who has not been seen in years and around whom local legend has built a terrifying mythology.

The children dare each other to approach the Radley house, leave notes for Boo, and act out stories about him. Meanwhile, they begin to find small gifts — a piece of gum, two pennies, carved soap figures of themselves — left in the knothole of a tree near the Radley property. Someone is watching over them, though they don't yet know who.

"You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view... Until you climb inside of his skin and walk around in it."
Chapter Summary: The Trial of Tom Robinson

Atticus is appointed to defend Tom Robinson, a Black man accused of raping Mayella Ewell, the daughter of the town's most despised white family. Despite knowing the case is nearly impossible to win in the racially charged atmosphere of 1930s Alabama, Atticus mounts a vigorous defense. He demonstrates that Tom's left arm is crippled — making it physically impossible for him to have committed the crime as described — and that Mayella's injuries were inflicted by a left-handed person, pointing to her abusive father Bob Ewell.

The evidence clearly points to Tom's innocence, but the all-white jury convicts him anyway. Scout and Jem watch from the "colored balcony" and are devastated by the verdict. Atticus's quiet dignity in the face of this injustice becomes a defining lesson for his children. Tom Robinson is later shot dead while attempting to escape prison.

"The one thing that doesn't abide by majority rule is a person's conscience."
Chapter Summary: Boo Radley's Gift

Bob Ewell, humiliated by Atticus's exposure of his lies in court, seeks revenge. On Halloween night, he attacks Scout and Jem as they walk home from a school pageant. Jem's arm is broken in the struggle. They are saved by a mysterious figure who carries Jem home — it is Boo Radley, who has been silently watching over the children all along. Bob Ewell is found dead, killed by Boo in defense of the children.

The sheriff decides to report that Ewell fell on his own knife, protecting Boo from public scrutiny. Scout understands: to drag Boo into the spotlight would be like shooting a mockingbird. She walks Boo home and, standing on his porch, finally sees the neighborhood through his eyes. She has grown up.

"Atticus, he was real nice." "Most people are, Scout, when you finally see them."
About the Author

Nelle Harper Lee (1926–2016) was born in Monroeville, Alabama, the youngest of four children of a lawyer father — widely believed to be the model for Atticus Finch. She studied law at the University of Alabama and later moved to New York City, where she worked as an airline reservations clerk while writing her novel. Her childhood friend Truman Capote is believed to be the inspiration for the character Dill Harris.

To Kill a Mockingbird was her first novel and became an immediate bestseller. It was adapted into an Academy Award-winning film in 1962, with Gregory Peck's portrayal of Atticus Finch becoming iconic. Lee published a second novel, Go Set a Watchman, in 2015, shortly before her death.

Themes and Analysis

Racial Injustice: The novel's central theme is the deep-rooted racial prejudice of the American South. Tom Robinson's conviction despite clear evidence of his innocence illustrates how systemic racism can override truth and justice.

Moral Courage: Atticus Finch represents moral courage — the willingness to do what is right even when it is unpopular or dangerous. He teaches his children that real courage is not physical bravado but the strength to stand by one's convictions.

Loss of Innocence: Scout and Jem's journey from childhood innocence to a more complex understanding of the adult world is central to the novel. They must confront the fact that the world is not always fair or just.

Empathy and Compassion: Atticus's most important lesson is the importance of empathy — of trying to understand others' perspectives before judging them. This lesson applies to Boo Radley as much as to Tom Robinson.
