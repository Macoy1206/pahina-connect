Of Mice and Men

by John Steinbeck

Overview

Published in 1937, Of Mice and Men is a short but powerful novella by John Steinbeck set during the Great Depression in California. It tells the story of two displaced migrant ranch workers — the quick-witted George Milton and his large, mentally disabled friend Lennie Small — who share a dream of one day owning their own land. The title comes from a Robert Burns poem: "The best-laid schemes o' mice an' men / Gang aft agley" (often go awry).

The novella is a tragedy about the impossibility of the American Dream for the poor and marginalized. It explores themes of friendship, loneliness, disability, and the cruelty of a world that has no place for the vulnerable. Despite its brevity, it is one of the most emotionally powerful works in American literature.

Published: 1937 | Genre: Tragedy, Social Realism | Setting: Salinas Valley, California, 1930s
Chapter Summary: The Dream

George and Lennie arrive at a ranch near Soledad, California, to work as barley buckers. George is small, sharp, and protective; Lennie is enormous, immensely strong, and has the mind of a child. He loves to pet soft things — mice, puppies, soft hair — but his great strength means he often accidentally kills what he loves. They have been run out of their last town after Lennie grabbed a woman's dress and wouldn't let go.

Their dream is simple and beautiful: to save enough money to buy a small farm where they can "live off the fatta the lan'" — grow their own food, keep rabbits (which Lennie especially loves), and be their own bosses. When they share this dream with the old, one-handed ranch hand Candy, he offers his life savings to join them. For a brief moment, the dream seems almost within reach.

"Guys like us, that work on ranches, are the loneliest guys in the world. They got no family. They don't belong no place... With us it ain't like that. We got a future."
Chapter Summary: Curley's Wife

The ranch is a place of loneliness and tension. Curley, the boss's son, is a small, aggressive man who picks fights with larger men to prove himself. His wife — never given a name — is lonely and flirtatious, wandering the ranch looking for company. The other men see her as trouble. Crooks, the Black stable hand, lives alone in the harness room, segregated from the others, and bitterly understands the cruelty of a world that denies him dignity.

Lennie, left alone in the barn, accidentally kills his puppy by petting it too hard. Curley's wife finds him and, feeling sorry for him, lets him stroke her soft hair. When she tells him to stop and he panics, he holds on too tightly and breaks her neck. He flees to the riverbank where George told him to go if he ever got in trouble.

"I never get to talk to nobody. I get awful lonely."
Chapter Summary: The Mercy of Friendship

When Curley's wife's body is discovered, Curley organizes a lynch mob. George knows what will happen to Lennie if Curley's men find him — a brutal, terrifying death. He finds Lennie at the riverbank first. He sits with him and, one last time, tells him about their dream — the farm, the rabbits, the life they were going to have. As Lennie looks out over the water, imagining it, George shoots him in the back of the head. It is an act of mercy and of love.

The other men arrive and assume George killed Lennie in self-defense. Only Slim understands what really happened. He puts his arm around George and leads him away. The dream is over. George is now just another lonely ranch hand, like all the others.

"Ever'body wants a little piece of lan'. I read plenty of books out here. Nobody never gets to heaven, and nobody gets no land."
About the Author

John Ernst Steinbeck Jr. (1902–1968) was born in Salinas, California — the same region where Of Mice and Men is set. He worked as a laborer alongside migrant workers in his youth, giving him firsthand knowledge of the lives he depicted in his fiction. He is best known for The Grapes of Wrath (1939), which won the Pulitzer Prize and depicted the Dust Bowl migration of Oklahoma farmers to California.

Steinbeck won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1962. His work is characterized by deep sympathy for the poor and working class, vivid descriptions of the California landscape, and a tragic sense of the gap between human dreams and human reality. Other notable works include East of Eden, Cannery Row, and Tortilla Flat.

Themes and Analysis

The American Dream: George and Lennie's dream of owning land represents the American Dream — the belief that hard work can lead to independence and security. The novella shows this dream to be cruelly out of reach for the poor and marginalized.

Loneliness and Companionship: Nearly every character in the novel is profoundly lonely. George and Lennie's friendship is presented as something rare and precious in a world where men like them typically have no one. Their bond is what makes the ending so devastating.

Disability and Vulnerability: Lennie's mental disability makes him dependent on George and vulnerable to a world that has no patience for those who are different. His tragedy is that his great physical strength is paired with an inability to understand the consequences of his actions.

Fate and Powerlessness: The title's allusion to Burns suggests that even the best-laid plans are subject to forces beyond our control. The characters are trapped by their circumstances — by poverty, by disability, by race, by gender — and their dreams are doomed from the start.
