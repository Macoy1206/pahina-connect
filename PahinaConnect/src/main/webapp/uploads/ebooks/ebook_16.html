Treasure Island

by Robert Louis Stevenson · 1883

Part I: The Old Sea Dog

Jim Hawkins is a boy who helps his parents run the Admiral Benbow Inn on the coast of England. One autumn, a weathered old sailor arrives and takes a room — a man who calls himself the Captain, who drinks rum, sings sea shanties, and keeps a nervous watch on the road as if expecting someone. He pays Jim a small sum to watch for a one-legged man and warn him if such a person appears.

The Captain is Billy Bones, a former pirate who is hiding from his old crew. When he dies of a stroke, Jim and his mother search his sea chest and find a packet of papers — including a map of an island with three red crosses marking the location of a buried treasure. The treasure belongs to the legendary pirate Captain Flint, and it is worth an enormous fortune.

Jim brings the map to the local squire, Trelawney, and the doctor, Livesey. The squire is immediately fired with excitement and begins organizing an expedition. He hires a ship, the Hispaniola, and recruits a crew — including, on the recommendation of a one-legged sea cook named Long John Silver, most of the crew. It is a decision that will nearly cost them all their lives.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part II: Long John Silver

Long John Silver is one of the great characters in adventure literature — charming, intelligent, funny, and utterly ruthless. He is a man of contradictions: genuinely fond of Jim, capable of real warmth and humor, and at the same time a cold-blooded pirate who will kill without hesitation when it serves his purposes. Jim likes him instinctively, and the reader likes him too, which makes him all the more dangerous.

Jim discovers the truth about Silver and the crew by accident — hiding in an apple barrel, he overhears Silver planning a mutiny. The pirates intend to let the honest men lead them to the treasure, then kill them all and take it for themselves. Jim brings this information to the captain and the squire, and they spend the rest of the voyage in a state of armed tension, waiting for the mutiny to begin.

When the ship reaches Treasure Island, the mutiny erupts. The honest men are outnumbered but not outmaneuvered. They take refuge in a stockade on the island and prepare to defend themselves. The battle for the treasure has begun.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part III: The Island

Treasure Island is a place of beauty and danger — lush forests, hidden coves, and the constant threat of violence. Jim, characteristically, cannot stay put. He slips away from the stockade on his own and has a series of adventures: he finds a marooned sailor named Ben Gunn, who has been living alone on the island for three years and who knows where the treasure is buried; he captures the Hispaniola from a drunken pirate; and he is captured by Silver and the pirates.

Silver, in a remarkable display of pragmatism, switches sides when it becomes clear that his men are losing. He protects Jim from the other pirates, calculating that Jim is his best chance of escaping with his life. The relationship between Jim and Silver — the boy and the pirate, the innocent and the experienced, the honest and the cunning — is the moral center of the novel.

The treasure, when they find it, is gone — Ben Gunn dug it up years ago. The pirates' rage is terrible, but the honest men are ready for them. The battle is won. The treasure is loaded onto the ship, and they sail for home.

✦ ✦ ✦

Part IV: The Voyage Home

Silver escapes during the voyage home, taking a small portion of the treasure with him. Jim is almost relieved — he could not have borne to see Silver hanged. The old pirate disappears into the world, and Jim never sees him again. But Silver haunts the novel's ending, a reminder that the world is not neatly divided into heroes and villains.

Jim returns home rich and changed. He has seen violence and death, has been tested and has not been found wanting. He has also met Long John Silver — a man who showed him that courage, intelligence, and charm can serve evil as easily as good, and that the most dangerous people are often the most likable.

✦ ✦ ✦

Epilogue: The Enduring Appeal of Adventure

Treasure Island has been thrilling readers for nearly a century and a half because Stevenson understood something fundamental about the appeal of adventure: it is not just about excitement, but about the testing of character. Jim Hawkins is not simply a boy who finds treasure — he is a boy who discovers what he is made of, who learns to think for himself, to act under pressure, and to judge people by their actions rather than their words.

Long John Silver endures as one of literature's great characters because he refuses to be simply a villain. He is a man of genuine gifts who chose to use them for evil — and who, in his relationship with Jim, shows that even the most hardened criminal retains some capacity for genuine human connection. Treasure Island is an adventure story, but it is also a story about the complexity of human nature.
